# Time Blocking Templates for Different Work Styles

*Originally published on [Schedule Calendar Blog](https://calendar-extension.site/blog/time-blocking-templates.html)*

---

Not all work schedules look the same, which is why most time blocking advice fails people with reactive, meeting-heavy, or remote-first roles. The solution is a template — a default pattern adapted to your specific work context.

For makers (engineers, writers, designers): a long morning block of 2–3 hours of uninterrupted focus, with all meetings clustered in the afternoon. The morning block is non-negotiable except for genuine emergencies.

For managers: accept that meetings happen throughout the day, and instead protect one reliable midday block. Cluster morning meetings to create a predictable afternoon window. The goal is not minimizing meetings — it is having one reliable focus slot.

For remote workers with async communication: front-load a 60-minute async catch-up (email, Slack, doc comments) before the focus block starts. By 9am, the communication backlog is handled and the focus block begins with a clear head.

For highly reactive roles (support, sales, recruiting): a single reliable anchor block — same time each day, 60 minutes — dedicated to the most important proactive work. The rest of the day responds to demand. The measure of success: does the anchor block happen at least four out of five days?

All templates share one principle: some time is protected, the rest is available. The ratio varies by role; the principle does not.
