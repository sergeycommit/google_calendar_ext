# 5 Time Blocking Mistakes That Kill Productivity

*Originally published on [Schedule Calendar Blog](https://calendar-extension.site/blog/time-blocking-mistakes.html)*

---

Time blocking stops working for predictable reasons. The most common is structural, not motivational.

Mistake one: blocking 100% of your hours. A fully-loaded calendar is one disruption away from total collapse. A meeting runs long, a task takes twice as expected, and suddenly the plan for the day is fiction. Blocking 60–70% of available hours gives the system room to absorb the unexpected.

Mistake two: vague names. A block called 'Work' gives no direction when you sit down at 9am. You still need to decide what to do, which means the block provides no real commitment. 'Draft intro for Q3 report' answers the question before you ask it.

Mistake three: Free status. A focus block set to Free accepts meeting invites without friction. One toggle in Google Calendar (setting it to Busy) changes the default from 'this time is open' to 'this time is taken.'

Mistake four: scheduling blocks in the worst slots. Deep work that lives in the tired post-lunch hours rarely produces good output. Blocking peak concentration time — even if that means being less available for meetings — is more honest about where output actually comes from.

Mistake five: treating blocks as permanent. Time blocking works best with a daily 5-minute review: does this block still reflect the right work for today? If something more important has emerged, move the block. Flexibility is part of the system.
