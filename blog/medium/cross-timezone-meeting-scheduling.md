# How to Schedule Across Time Zones Without the Chaos

*Originally published on [Schedule Calendar Blog](https://calendar-extension.site/blog/cross-timezone-meeting-scheduling.html)*

---

Cross-timezone scheduling fails in the same ways repeatedly, and most of those failures are preventable. The three most common are: communicating meeting times without a timezone qualifier, scheduling at the organizer's preferred time without examining the other party's hours, and failing to account for daylight saving time transitions that shift the hour difference twice a year.

The simplest preventive measure is the timezone qualifier habit. Writing '3 PM' without a zone marker is ambiguous for anyone in a different city. Writing '3 PM EST / 8 PM GMT' removes the ambiguity at the cost of five characters. For recurring meeting communications, specifying the anchor timezone — the zone that defines the meeting time, which may differ from attendees' zones — also helps when daylight saving transitions shift the effective time.

Before sending a cross-timezone meeting invite, using a visual overlap tool saves the back-and-forth of proposing and rejecting times. World Time Buddy, Every Time Zone, and Google Calendar's own world clock sidebar all let you see, at a glance, what 2 PM in New York looks like for someone in Berlin, Tokyo, or São Paulo. For common timezone pairs, the overlap window is usually obvious once visualized. For difficult pairs like US West Coast and East Asia, the visualization makes the absence of a natural window clear — which is a useful prompt to evaluate whether the meeting needs to be synchronous at all.

For regular cross-timezone team meetings, establishing a rotation norm for the inconvenient time slot reduces the quiet resentment that builds when one person always takes the 7 AM or 9 PM call. Acknowledging the inconvenience explicitly in the invite — 'I know this is outside your normal hours' — does not solve the problem but signals awareness and care.
