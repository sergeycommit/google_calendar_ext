# Why 15-Minute Meetings Work Better Than 30

*Originally published on [Schedule Calendar Blog](https://calendar-extension.site/blog/15-minute-meeting-format.html)*

---

The 30-minute meeting is not a result of research into human cognition or meeting effectiveness. It is a default built into calendar software because scheduling in 30-minute increments is convenient. Parkinson's Law fills the rest: a meeting expands to occupy whatever time is blocked for it.

A 15-minute meeting is not a rushed version of a 30-minute meeting. It is a structurally different format that works well for specific types of conversations — and fails badly for others.

It works when the purpose is singular and specific. A decision that needs one person's input to unblock a project. A quick alignment check before a document goes to the client. A daily standup where each person shares three sentences. A short debrief after an event before details fade. These conversations benefit from the constraint because the constraint prevents scope creep and off-topic discussion.

The format requires preparation that the 30-minute default often lets people skip. When you have 15 minutes, you cannot spend the first ten reconstructing context. Every attendee needs to arrive knowing what the meeting is for and what they need to contribute.

Structure the fifteen minutes deliberately: thirty seconds of purpose statement, ten minutes on the substance, three to four minutes on next steps and owners. State who is responsible for what before the meeting ends — not in a follow-up email.

End on time every time. This is where the format earns its credibility. A 15-minute meeting that reliably ends at fifteen minutes builds trust in a way that meetings that run over routinely do not. When the time limit is known to be real, attendees prepare more, stay focused, and do not hold back their most important point for minute twenty-eight.
