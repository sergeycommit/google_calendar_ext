# How to Use the Pomodoro Technique With Google Calendar

*Originally published on [Schedule Calendar Blog](https://calendar-extension.site/blog/pomodoro-google-calendar.html)*

---

The Pomodoro Technique is a simple productivity system: 25 minutes of uninterrupted focus, followed by a 5-minute break, repeated four times before a longer rest. Its power comes from making the commitment to focus feel finite — 25 minutes is short enough to start without procrastination, long enough to produce meaningful work.

On its own, the technique manages time within a session. Combined with Google Calendar, it becomes a system for protecting those sessions from the scheduling pressure that erodes them.

The calendar's role in a Pomodoro-based workflow is not to track individual 25-minute intervals — that is the timer's job. The calendar protects the larger block that contains those intervals. A 50 or 90-minute focus event on your calendar, marked as busy, signals to anyone who schedules with you that this time is committed. Without that calendar block, your peak focus hours remain available for meeting invitations.

Creating recurring Pomodoro blocks during your typical high-focus hours — mornings for most people — transforms an intention into a structure. A recurring 90-minute 'Deep work' block at 9 AM, five days a week, gives you the protected space to run two full Pomodoro cycles before the meeting-heavy part of the day begins.

Naming blocks specifically adds accountability. A block labeled 'Writing: product spec' is both more visible to colleagues (they understand why it is protected) and more honest with yourself. At the end of the week, reviewing how many of those labeled blocks ran as planned — versus how many were cancelled for meetings or other reasons — gives you clear data about your actual work patterns.
