# Google Calendar for Freelancers: Billing Time and Client Calls

*Originally published on [Schedule Calendar Blog](https://calendar-extension.site/blog/google-calendar-for-freelancers.html)*

---

Freelancers need more from their calendar than most people realize. A standard employee calendar tracks meetings and blocks focus time. A freelancer's calendar also needs to reflect which client owns which work sessions, when invoices are due, where proposals need to land, and whether business development is actually getting scheduled or getting crowded out by client delivery work.

The foundation of a useful freelance calendar is client-level color coding. Create a separate Google Calendar for each active client and assign it a distinct color. Every event related to that client — meetings, work sessions, milestone deadlines — lives in that calendar. In week view, the color distribution gives an instant visual picture of which clients are taking the most time and whether the allocation matches the revenue they generate.

Billable work sessions deserve calendar blocks even when no meeting is involved. A block labeled 'Client A: homepage copy draft' from 10 AM to noon, in Client A's calendar color, does two things. First, it protects that time from other clients' meeting requests. Second, it creates a lightweight time log that makes invoice preparation easier and more accurate. Reconstructing billable hours from memory at the end of the month is error-prone; reviewing a month of calendar blocks is not.

Milestones and invoice dates as all-day events on their due dates provide persistent visibility. A 'Client B: project delivery' all-day event on Friday reminds you when the week's primary constraint is without blocking any time slot.

Business development — proposals, prospecting calls, networking, existing client relationship work beyond the active project — needs protected calendar time. For most freelancers, this is the first thing to get squeezed when client load increases. Since client load is highest when business development matters least (you are busy) and lowest when it matters most (you need work), protecting a consistent weekly block for this activity regardless of current load is a structural safeguard against the feast-famine cycle.
