# When to Kill a Recurring Meeting (and How)

*Originally published on [Schedule Calendar Blog](https://calendar-extension.site/blog/recurring-meeting-audit.html)*

---

Recurring meetings are the most overlooked category on most knowledge workers' calendars. They were created at a specific moment, for a specific purpose, and then continued automatically long after that purpose was resolved or changed.

The mechanism is simple: creating a recurring meeting takes one action. Canceling it requires noticing it has become unnecessary, which requires stepping back from the routine long enough to evaluate it. Most people never do. The meeting runs until someone leaves the team or the calendar gets audited.

A recurring meeting audit takes about twenty minutes and often recovers hours per week. The process is: list every recurring meeting you attend or own, and for each one ask three questions. What was the original purpose? Is that purpose still active? What would actually break if this meeting stopped tomorrow?

The third question is the most revealing. For many recurring meetings, the honest answer is 'nothing.' The information shared in the meeting could flow through a written update. The alignment achieved could happen through a shared document. The decisions made are so infrequent they could happen in an ad-hoc call when genuinely needed.

For meetings you own, you have direct authority to cancel or reshape them. A short message to attendees — what the meeting accomplished, why it is ending, and what replaces it — turns the cancellation into a transition rather than an abandonment.

For meetings you attend but do not own, a private conversation with the organizer works better than raising the topic in the meeting itself. Framing it as curiosity rather than critique — 'I wanted to check in about what this meeting is currently providing' — opens the door to a productive redesign without putting the organizer on the defensive.

Build this audit into your quarterly planning. It takes less time than any individual meeting it eliminates, and the dividends compound every week afterward.
