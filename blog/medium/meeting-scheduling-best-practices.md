# 6 Rules for Smarter Meeting Scheduling

*Originally published on [Schedule Calendar Blog](https://calendar-extension.site/blog/meeting-scheduling-best-practices.html)*

---

Smarter meeting scheduling does not require a team policy or a cultural overhaul. It requires six habits applied consistently by the person who owns the meeting. Each rule addresses a specific failure mode that turns otherwise productive teams into calendar-management nightmares.

Start with purpose. Before you open your calendar tool, write one sentence about what the meeting needs to accomplish. Not what will be discussed — what will be decided, agreed, or produced. If you cannot write that sentence, you are not ready to schedule the meeting yet. This check alone eliminates a surprising number of unnecessary meetings.

Default to shorter. Change your calendar's default meeting durations: 30 becomes 25, 60 becomes 50. These five-to-ten minute gaps give people time to reach the next call without being late, write a quick note, and close the previous context before opening the new one. The shorter default also introduces light time pressure that keeps meetings from wandering.

Invite fewer people. Ask yourself: who has a speaking role or a decision to make? Everyone else can receive a written recap. Larger groups feel inclusive but produce slower decisions and less engaged participation from the people on the edges of the invite list.

Time it thoughtfully. Most knowledge workers have windows of peak performance — for many, this is mid-morning. Scheduling a meeting during that window costs more than scheduling it in a lower-energy slot. When you have discretion over timing, choose mid-morning or early afternoon.

Always include an agenda. One sentence is enough. It signals that the meeting has a purpose, tells people what to prepare, and gives you a tool for steering the conversation when it drifts.

Give notice. Scheduling a meeting for an hour from now is usually a favor to the organizer and a disruption for everyone else. Twenty-four hours of notice is a basic standard. For complex topics, 48 hours gives people enough time to do genuine preparation.
