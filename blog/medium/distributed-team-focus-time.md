# Protecting Focus Time on Distributed Teams

*Originally published on [Schedule Calendar Blog](https://calendar-extension.site/blog/distributed-team-focus-time.html)*

---

Protecting focus time is harder on a distributed team than in a co-located setting, and harder still than individually. The challenge is not just personal discipline — it is the combination of cross-timezone scheduling pressure, persistent async communication tools, and the absence of the physical cues that signal 'this person is in deep work' in an office.

The individual practices for focus time protection apply: block recurring focus events in your calendar, mark them busy, schedule them during peak cognitive hours. For distributed team members, one additional step makes these blocks more effective — making them legible across timezones. A block labeled 'Focus: engineering work' with a brief note in the event that this is a protected deep work period communicates the block's intent to colleagues in other locations who might otherwise interpret an empty-looking window as available time.

The more durable protection comes from team-level norms rather than individual enforcement. A team agreement that a specific daily window is no-meeting time changes the default scheduling behavior for everyone. Meeting organizers stop looking at that window as an option; team members stop needing to defend it meeting by meeting. The norm does the work that individual willpower cannot sustain consistently.

Designing the synchronous overlap window with focus time in mind is the structural solution. If the team's viable meeting window consistently falls during peak cognitive hours for one location, that location's deep work time is structurally at risk. Adjusting when the overlap window falls — even by an hour — can preserve morning focus time for locations where that is most valuable, at the cost of a slightly different meeting cadence.
