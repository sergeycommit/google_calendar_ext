# Time Boxing vs. Time Blocking: Key Differences Explained

*Originally published on [Schedule Calendar Blog](https://calendar-extension.site/blog/time-boxing-vs-time-blocking.html)*

---

Time blocking and time boxing are often used interchangeably. They are not the same.

Time blocking reserves a calendar slot for a task. When you finish, you stop — or continue if you need more time. The block defines when you work; completion determines when you stop.

Time boxing adds a hard constraint: the task ends when the box does, regardless of progress. You work for the defined period and stop at the end, even if the work is incomplete.

This difference matters most for tasks that tend to expand. Email can fill any amount of time given to it. A 30-minute timebox on email check-up prevents it from consuming the morning. A time block without a hard endpoint for email is a different thing — it becomes a vague commitment with no natural close.

Conversely, a timebox on a complex creative task can interrupt flow at exactly the wrong moment. A design session, a complex piece of writing, a difficult engineering problem — these benefit from a block that allows the work to reach its natural completion.

The practical combination: use timeboxes for shallow, administrative, and open-ended tasks (email, Slack, planning sessions, reviews). Use time blocks for work with meaningful completion points (drafts, designs, code, analysis).

Both appear as calendar events. The difference is what you do when the time ends.
