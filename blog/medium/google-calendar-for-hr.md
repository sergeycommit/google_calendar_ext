# HR Calendar Management: Onboarding, Reviews, and Recruiting

*Originally published on [Schedule Calendar Blog](https://calendar-extension.site/blog/google-calendar-for-hr.html)*

---

HR work follows cycles — quarterly reviews, annual open enrollment, bi-annual compliance training, monthly new hire cohort onboarding. Each cycle has a predictable structure: a preparation phase, an execution phase, and a close-out phase. A calendar system built around these cycles converts reactive scramble into repeatable process.

The most impactful HR calendar practice is front-loading the annual cycle at the start of each year. In January (or whenever the year begins for your organization), add every major HR process to the calendar: performance review windows, open enrollment dates, compliance training deadlines, engagement survey periods, and any company-wide HR programs. These all-day events appear in the calendar throughout the year, creating natural preparation cues weeks before each process begins.

Onboarding calendaring deserves special attention. A new hire's first thirty, sixty, and ninety days follow a structure that can be templated. Creating a standard set of calendar events — orientation on day one, manager check-in at end of week one, role milestone reviews at thirty and sixty days, probationary review at ninety days — and sending them to each new hire at the start of their first week creates a visible structure for the onboarding experience. The calendar commitment is significantly more likely to be honored than a reminder that depends on someone remembering to schedule it.

Recruiting creates a specific scheduling challenge: interview loops require coordinating multiple interviewers' availability, often under time pressure as candidates evaluate multiple offers simultaneously. Blocking recurring interview availability windows in hiring managers' calendars — rather than scheduling ad-hoc for each candidate — creates predictable slots that reduce scheduling coordination time from days to hours.

The calendar's role in HR is partly accountability: when the 60-day check-in is on the calendar from day one, it happens. When it depends on a reminder that might be forgotten, it often does not.
