# Weekly Planning Ritual: A Minimal Implementation That Actually Works

*Hacker News post · November 14, 2027*

---

Weekly planning advice tends toward elaborate systems: themed days, personal OKR reviews, full GTD processing, rolling 13-week outlooks. These are interesting but have high adoption overhead and low completion rates.

Here's a 20-minute version that captures most of the value with minimal overhead.

## The minimal weekly review

**Time: 20 minutes, Sunday evening or Friday afternoon.**

**Step 1: Open the calendar, read next week. (5 min)**
All meetings, all blocks, all commitments. Look for: obvious overloads, meetings I need to prepare for, conflicts I haven't resolved.

**Step 2: Identify the three most important outcomes. (5 min)**
Not tasks — outcomes. "Have a working draft of the auth spec" vs "write auth spec section 1." Three is a deliberate limit; more than three dilutes the question. If everything is important, nothing is.

**Step 3: Block time for the three outcomes. (5 min)**
For each outcome: find a window in next week's calendar, create a block, name it specifically. If there's no window, that's information: the outcome isn't realistic for this week or you need to move something.

**Step 4: Resolve any conflicts. (5 min)**
Two meetings that overlap. A deadline that requires more prep time than available. A colleague's scheduling request you've been ignoring. Resolve now, when the decision is low-stakes, not 30 minutes before it becomes high-stakes.

## What this doesn't cover

Full task list review, project status, long-horizon planning, retrospective on last week. These are valuable but not required for the minimal version to work.

Adding them is easy: extend to 35 minutes and add whatever components you actually use. Subtract anything you add but consistently skip.

## The failure mode to avoid

Over-engineering the planning session to be longer, more comprehensive, and more satisfying as a planning activity — at the expense of actually doing the work. If you're spending 90 minutes planning a week and the plan rarely survives Tuesday, the system is optimizing for planning, not for output.
