# Reducing Meeting Overload: Auditing and Restructuring Without Organizational Buy-In

*Hacker News post · September 20, 2026*

---

Meeting overload is structurally produced. Meetings accumulate because adding one is easy and removing one is awkward. No one audits the total. Individual cost-benefit analysis is skewed because the person scheduling the meeting sees its value clearly (to them) and distributes its cost across all attendees.

Reducing meeting load doesn't require organizational policy change. Individual-level interventions are effective.

## The individual audit

Every quarter: open your recurring meeting list. Apply one filter to each item: "What specifically breaks if this stops?"

Not "is this valuable?" — everything has value. The question forces you to name the specific, concrete downstream effect of removal.

The answer often reveals three categories:
- **Load-bearing:** Something specific breaks. Keep it.
- **Reducible:** The value exists but the frequency or duration is higher than the requirement. Reduce.
- **Inertial:** It continues because nobody has stopped it. Cancel.

In typical recurring meeting lists, 20-30% of items fall into the third category.

## The language for reducing

"Can we try moving this to bi-weekly for a month and see if we lose anything?" is lower friction than "I'm cancelling this." The trial framing shifts from permanent loss to experiment. Most experiments confirm that the reduced frequency is fine.

For meetings you attend but didn't organize: "I've been thinking about what I actually contribute in this meeting — would it be okay to try skipping for a few weeks and seeing if anything breaks?" Works surprisingly often.

## The policy angle (when you have influence)

Team-wide meeting norms require more leverage but scale better. "No meetings before 10am" or "Wednesday mornings are async" are policies that protect the whole team, not just individuals who are willing to push back.

These require proposing explicitly and usually benefit from a trial framing: "Let's try this for six weeks and measure output."
