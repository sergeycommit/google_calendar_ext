# Common Time Blocking Failures: A Systematic Analysis

*Hacker News post · June 14, 2026*

---

Time blocking has a high dropout rate. People try it, it doesn't produce the expected results, they conclude it doesn't work for them. Having observed this pattern frequently, I think the failure modes are systematic and predictable — not personal.

## The five failure modes, in order of frequency

**1. Over-blocking (most common)**

Scheduling 90%+ of available hours leaves no slack for the unexpected. A single unexpected meeting or task that takes longer than estimated cascades through the rest of the day. The plan breaks, feels bad, and the system gets blamed rather than the specific planning error.

Fix: 60-70% target. The remaining 30-40% absorbs the unexpected.

**2. Vague block names**

"Deep work" is not a plan. When the block arrives and you sit down to work, the absence of a specific task creates the opening for the inbox. The specific task name ("Implement OAuth callback handler") removes that decision point.

Fix: task-specific names, required.

**3. Free status on blocks**

This is an infrastructure error. A Free block accepts meeting invites silently. The block exists but offers no resistance. Within days, meetings fill the "available" time, and the block doesn't register as a conflict.

Fix: Busy status on all work blocks.

**4. Wrong time placement**

Placing demanding work in low-energy time (post-lunch, late afternoon) produces poor results. The block ran; the output didn't match the effort. This gets attributed to distraction or lack of discipline.

Fix: match block type to energy level. Peak cognitive demand requires peak cognitive time.

**5. No review cycle**

The system runs for two weeks, breaks down, and gets abandoned. Without a review mechanism, you can't distinguish "system is working but this week was unusually disrupted" from "system has a structural problem."

Fix: weekly retrospective on which blocks ran and which didn't. Patterns emerge quickly.
