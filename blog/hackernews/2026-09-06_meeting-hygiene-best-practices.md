# 8 Meeting Hygiene Practices That Prevent Calendar Waste

*Hacker News post · September 6, 2026*

---

Meeting quality is determined more by inputs (preparation, structure, invite list) than by participants' meeting skills. Poorly structured meetings produce poor outcomes regardless of who attends. Well-structured meetings produce good outcomes with almost any team.

"Meeting hygiene" is the set of inputs you can control as an organizer or participant.

## The 8 practices

**1. Agenda, 24 hours before.** Even one sentence: "We need to decide X by the end of this meeting." This forces a clarity check: if you can't write an agenda, the meeting isn't ready.

**2. Named owner.** One person responsible for outcomes, decisions, and follow-through. Meetings without an owner produce diffuse accountability, which means no accountability.

**3. Stated meeting type.** Decision / brainstorm / update / review. Different types require different participant behavior. Stating it upfront aligns expectations.

**4. Invite list discipline.** Every attendee should have a clear role. "Stay informed" is a reason to receive meeting notes, not to attend the meeting. Each extra attendee has a coordination cost and a decision-making dilution cost.

**5. Start on time.** Not "when everyone joins." Late starts signal that late arrival is acceptable. The second meeting you start on time, the culture changes.

**6. Live notes document.** Decisions recorded as they happen, not from memory afterward. Attendees can see what's being captured and correct it in real time.

**7. Explicit close.** Decisions made, action items with owners and deadlines, named next steps. Not "let's wrap up" — specific closure.

**8. Written follow-up, within 2 hours.** Memory degrades quickly. A follow-up sent within two hours captures accurate state; one sent the next day captures what people remember wanting to have decided.

## The highest-leverage single change

If you implement only one: write an agenda before every meeting you organize. The act of writing it forces the clarity check. If you can't write it, the meeting isn't ready and shouldn't be scheduled yet.
