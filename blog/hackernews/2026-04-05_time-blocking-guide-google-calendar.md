# Time Blocking in Google Calendar: A Practical Setup Guide

*Hacker News post · April 5, 2026*

---

Time blocking — assigning calendar slots to specific tasks rather than just meetings — has a reputation for being either obvious or impractical. The obvious objection: "I already know I need to do the work." The impractical objection: "Meetings move, the blocks break, and I've wasted time on a plan."

Both objections are partially right. Here's what actually works in practice.

## The core setup

Three event types are sufficient for most knowledge workers:

**Deep work blocks (90–120 min):** For work requiring sustained concentration. Mark as Busy. Name specifically — "Draft API documentation v2", not "Deep work". The specific name creates weak accountability; the Busy status creates weak friction against meeting invites.

**Shallow work batches (30–45 min):** Email, Slack, admin. Two windows per day, outside peak hours. Not marked Busy — these can yield to urgent meetings without much cost.

**Buffer blocks (15 min):** Between dense meeting clusters. Context-switching costs are real; this absorbs them.

## The 60-70% rule

Blocking 100% of your calendar is a planning anti-pattern. When anything changes (and something always changes), the whole plan cascades. 60-70% blocking leaves structural slack that absorbs the unexpected without requiring you to re-plan the week.

## What changes with this approach

The main behavioral shift: you stop treating your calendar as a record of meetings and start treating it as a plan for where your time goes. That's not a big change technically. The scheduling mechanics are identical to creating a meeting. The difference is psychological: the work becomes visible alongside meetings, which makes trade-offs legible.

When someone asks "can you do Tuesday at 10?" and you have a deep work block there, you can make an informed decision — not just accept because you don't see a meeting conflict.
