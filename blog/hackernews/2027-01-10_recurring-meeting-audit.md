# Recurring Meeting Audit: A Systematic Process for Calendar Debt Reduction

*Hacker News post · January 10, 2027*

---

Recurring meetings are calendar debt. Each one was created for a reason. Many outlive that reason. Nobody removes them because the social cost of removal is perceived as higher than the ongoing time cost of attendance.

An audit makes this cost legible and provides a structured method for addressing it.

## The audit process

**Step 1: Export the full recurring meeting list.**

In Google Calendar, open Settings → View all calendars → My calendars. Look at recurring events under each calendar. Alternatively, search for your own email address as organizer and filter by recurring.

**Step 2: Apply the accountability filter.**

For each recurring event: "Who notices if this doesn't happen, and specifically what breaks for them?"

The qualifier "specifically" is doing work here. "We'd lose alignment" is not a specific answer. "The engineering team wouldn't know about API change decisions until they're already shipped" is specific. Vague value claims don't pass the filter.

**Step 3: Categorize each meeting.**

- **Essential:** Specific, concrete downstream effect if removed. Keep.
- **Frequency-reducible:** The value exists but happens less frequently than the current cadence requires. Reduce from weekly to bi-weekly, or monthly to quarterly.
- **Scope-reducible:** The meeting serves a purpose but runs too long or includes too many people.
- **Removable:** Exists on inertia. No specific answer to "what breaks."

**Step 4: Act on each category.**

For removable meetings: "I'm going to pause this for a month to see what we miss" is lower friction than "I'm cancelling this." Most paused meetings never resume.

For reducible meetings: propose the change explicitly. "Can we try bi-weekly for the next two months?" Most people say yes.

## The aggregate effect

In my experience auditing my own recurring meetings quarterly: 20-25% fall into the removable category, 30-35% are reducible. That's 50-60% of recurring meeting load available for reduction without losing any load-bearing coordination.
