# Shallow Work Batching: Reducing the Cognitive Cost of Communication Overhead

*Hacker News post · August 9, 2026*

---

There's a well-documented attention cost to task switching. Each context switch between different types of work incurs a brief (but non-trivial) recovery period — often estimated at 15-23 minutes to fully regain focus depth. If you're switching between focused work and email/Slack ten times a day, you're paying this cost ten times.

Shallow work batching is the practice of consolidating communication and administrative tasks into specific windows, reducing the number of switches to two or three per day.

## The mechanism

Instead of processing email when it arrives (reactive), you designate specific time windows — say, 10:30–11:00 and 4:00–4:30 — for all communication and admin. Outside these windows, notifications are off or ignored. Inside them, you process everything available.

The key effect: every focus session runs without the interruption of an incoming notification or the anxiety of wondering what arrived. The cognitive load of "is there something I should be responding to?" disappears for the focused period.

## The efficiency gain within the batch

Counter-intuitive result: email handled in a 45-minute batch is processed faster than the same email handled across 15 micro-interruptions throughout the day. The reason is context: you don't have to reload the context of who this person is and what the thread is about each time you return to it. You process all of it in one sitting with maintained context.

## Implementation tradeoffs

**Response latency increases.** If you batch twice daily, someone who sends you an email at 11am won't get a response until ~4pm. For most communication, this is fine. For time-sensitive work, you may need to carve out exceptions or communicate your response window to regular contacts.

**Async-vs-sync expectations.** Batching works better in cultures with async-communication norms. In high-urgency environments where "immediate response" is the norm, you're fighting a cultural expectation that batching alone can't resolve.

**Depth work quality improves measurably.** Most people who run this for two weeks report a noticeable improvement in the quality of output during focused sessions — not just the quantity.
