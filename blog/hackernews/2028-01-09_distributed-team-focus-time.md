# Ask HN: How Do Distributed Teams Protect Focus Time?

*Hacker News post · January 9, 2028*

---

Focus time protection in distributed teams is a harder problem than in co-located teams. The mechanisms that work in offices (visible signals of "I'm working," physical presence that conveys "don't interrupt") don't translate to remote environments.

Here's what we've found actually works, from running a distributed team ourselves and talking with others who have.

## The core problem

On a distributed team, "heads-down" is invisible. A colleague in a different city can't see that you're in deep concentration — they see only that you're online and presumably available. The implicit "don't interrupt unless urgent" signal from office body language doesn't exist.

The result: communication interruptions arrive regardless of cognitive state, because there's no shared signal system for "I'm in focus mode right now."

## Solutions that work at the individual level

**Explicit focus status.** Slack status set to "Focus mode until 11am" or custom emoji. This creates a shared signal that's respected in cultures that have established the norm. Requires repeated enforcement before it becomes ambient.

**Calendar blocks with visibility.** Work blocks marked with event titles visible to the team ("Deep work: auth system architecture") communicate what's happening without requiring a separate communication. Only works if the team has calendar visibility enabled.

**Designated response windows.** "I'll respond to Slack between 12-1 and 4-5" communicated once per project or team context. This converts the implicit expectation of immediate response to an explicit expectation of windowed response.

## Solutions that work at the team level

**Shared quiet hours.** A team policy that certain hours are high-focus — no meetings, slow Slack expectations. Requires explicit agreement, not assumption.

**The "two-hour rule."** Any cross-team communication gets a 2-hour expected response time unless explicitly marked urgent. This creates structural space for focus without requiring individual enforcement.

**"Focus Friday mornings."** One session per week designated team-wide for deep work. Predictability makes it easier to respect.

## What doesn't work

Individual calendar blocks without team norms. If your team hasn't agreed on focus time norms, your individual block just means you'll decline more invites and have more scheduling conversations. The structural benefit requires team-level adoption.
