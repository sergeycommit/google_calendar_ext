# How to Prevent Meetings From Consuming All Your Calendar Time

*Hacker News post · May 17, 2026*

---

The default state of most knowledge worker calendars is meeting-driven: meetings get scheduled first, work happens in the gaps. This is backwards. The gaps between meetings are the worst environment for focused work — they're too short, too fragmented, and already colored by the cognitive residue of the preceding meeting.

A practical reframe: work blocks are scheduled first. Meetings fill around them.

## Why the default is backwards

When meetings are scheduled first, you get leftover time for work. Leftover time properties: irregular durations (30–45 min), unpredictable, no lead-in or context, often follows a demanding meeting. Focused work in these conditions produces fraction of the output it would produce in a protected 90-minute block.

## The structural inversion

The principle: before accepting any new meeting request, check your work blocks first, not just your meeting schedule. A Busy work block is as valid a conflict as a meeting.

In practice: schedule your critical work blocks at the start of the week, before meeting requests arrive. If you know Tuesday morning requires a 2-hour concentrated coding session, put it on the calendar Sunday night. When Tuesday's meeting requests arrive, they'll see it.

## Meeting clustering as the secondary fix

Once work blocks are protected, clustering meetings into specific days or half-days reduces context-switching. Two meeting-heavy days per week (Tuesday/Thursday is common) creates three days with potentially clean mornings.

This requires some willingness to redirect meeting requests ("I'm focused on project work Monday/Wednesday/Friday — can we find Tuesday or Thursday?") but this conversation is surprisingly low-friction in practice.

## What this looks like to other people

A calendar that has work blocks marked Busy looks like a full calendar. Meeting organizers see the conflicts and either find another time or ask explicitly. The key is consistency — when every week's morning blocks are populated before the meeting wave arrives, the pattern becomes understood.
