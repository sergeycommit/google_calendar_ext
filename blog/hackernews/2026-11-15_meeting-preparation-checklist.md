# Meeting Preparation: What the 5 Minutes Before a Meeting Should Accomplish

*Hacker News post · November 15, 2026*

---

Most meetings start cold. Participants join without reviewing the agenda, without locating the relevant context document, and without thinking about what they want to accomplish. The first 10-15 minutes of a 60-minute meeting often go to orientation that preparation would have eliminated.

5 minutes of deliberate preparation before a meeting eliminates most of this dead time and changes what the meeting is capable of producing.

## The preparation checklist

**Read the agenda.** Even if you wrote it. Refresh: what is the specific outcome of this meeting? Not "discuss X" but "decide X" or "align on Y" or "produce Z."

**Write 2-3 key points.** Things you need to say, raise, or decide. Written before you join, not recalled 20 minutes after the meeting ends. This prevents the post-meeting regret of forgetting to raise an important point.

**Open the relevant document.** Whatever context material will be referenced — the spec, the proposal, the data — open it before you join. Doing this during the meeting interrupts your presence.

**Close your current work.** A 3-minute buffer to write down where you were on your current task (so you can return to it cleanly) and shift your attention to the meeting. This is context-switching overhead you cannot eliminate but can manage.

**Review attendees (for unknown meetings).** Who else is in this meeting? What roles do they have? What would be the most useful thing you could contribute given who's in the room?

## The structural implementation

The most reliable implementation: a 10-minute "buffer" block before any important meeting. Mark it as time for preparation. This makes the preparation explicit rather than relying on remembering to do it.

For routine recurring meetings, 3-minute prep is sufficient. For high-stakes presentations, reviews, or negotiations, 30 minutes is appropriate.

## Why the investment is worth it

A 5-minute preparation investment that eliminates 10 minutes of orientation time produces a net gain even at a 1:2 input-output ratio. It also compounds: meetings that run efficiently build a culture of efficient meetings.
