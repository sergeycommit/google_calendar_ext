# The 15-Minute Meeting Format: Constraint as Quality Signal

*Hacker News post · December 13, 2026*

---

Reducing a standard standup from 30 to 15 minutes sounds like it would reduce meeting quality. In practice, it often improves it. The mechanism is Parkinson's Law applied specifically to meetings: a meeting will expand to fill its allocated time.

## The information-density argument

A 30-minute standup processes a fixed amount of necessary information — blockers, priorities, dependencies — plus variable amounts of status theater and social padding. The necessary information typically takes 10-15 minutes. The remaining time fills with elaboration, tangents, and extended discussions that don't need the whole group.

A 15-minute hard limit forces ruthless prioritization: only blockers and direct interdependencies get air time. Everything else gets scheduled as a separate conversation with the relevant people only, rather than consuming the whole group's time.

## The structural implementation

**Pre-standup async update.** Each person posts a 2-3 sentence async update (yesterday / today / blockers) before the standup. The synchronous meeting becomes entirely about the blockers and dependencies — not status reporting, which is now already done.

**Strict 15-minute timer.** Not "we try to keep it short." An actual timer. When it hits 15 minutes, the meeting ends. Topics that didn't get to are addressed in separate conversations.

**"Take it offline" as the default.** Any discussion that isn't a blocker for the majority of attendees gets moved: "Let's take that offline with [person A] and [person B]." This is a norm, not an exception.

## When 15 minutes isn't enough

Projects with high interdependency and frequent blocking situations genuinely need more. The 15-minute format works best when:
- The team has a functioning async update rhythm
- Most standups are coordination, not problem-solving
- The team is willing to have follow-on conversations

If standups consistently run over 15 minutes despite the constraint, that's signal: the team has too many blockers and the standup isn't the right venue for resolving them.
