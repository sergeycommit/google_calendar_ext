# Async Team Scheduling: Practical Patterns for Distributed Teams

*Hacker News post · January 23, 2028*

---

"Async-first" is easy to say and hard to do consistently. The failure mode: teams declare async intent but default to sync meetings when ambiguity arises, because meetings feel like they resolve uncertainty faster (they often don't).

Here are the patterns that make async scheduling actually work at the team level.

## The async-appropriate communication taxonomy

Not everything benefits equally from async treatment. The ROI of async increases as:
- Geographic/timezone distribution increases
- Information complexity increases (more context to convey)
- Time sensitivity decreases
- Audience size increases (cost of synchronizing N people scales poorly)

The ROI of async decreases as:
- Emotional stakes increase (tone matters; text loses it)
- Iteration rounds multiply (a 10-message async thread on a simple decision is worse than a 10-minute sync)
- Real-time collaboration would produce better output (some work is genuinely better done together)

## The patterns

**Decision docs with deadlines.** For decisions requiring input from multiple people: a shared doc with the proposal, options considered, and a decision deadline ("I need input by Thursday EOD"). People contribute on their schedule; the decision gets made at the deadline regardless of who responded. Reduces "alignment meetings" by 60-80% for teams that adopt this.

**Pre-read async + short sync.** For complex topics: send a detailed async context doc 48 hours before a meeting. The meeting becomes a 20-minute decision session rather than a 60-minute catch-up. Requires the pre-read to be done.

**Standing async updates.** Weekly async "what I'm working on / what I'm blocked by" in Slack or a shared doc. Replaces the status-update standup entirely for teams with good async discipline.

**Async review with explicit question.** "I've attached the proposal. The specific question I need input on is X. If you don't have a perspective on this specific question, you don't need to respond." This filters out the social obligation to respond to every shared document.

## The calendar implications

Async-first teams have fundamentally different calendars. Fewer recurring meetings. More focus blocks. Meeting time concentrated in slots that are more convenient for more people. The calendar becomes a planning tool rather than a meeting schedule.
