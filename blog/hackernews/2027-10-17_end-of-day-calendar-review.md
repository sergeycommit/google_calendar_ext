# End-of-Day Calendar Review: A 5-Minute Practice That Prevents Scheduling Errors

*Hacker News post · October 17, 2027*

---

Most scheduling errors are recoverable if you catch them with sufficient lead time. A meeting conflict at 9am is easily resolved if you discover it at 5pm the previous day. It's unresolvable if you discover it at 8:55am.

An end-of-day calendar review is a 5-minute practice that catches tomorrow's problems today.

## What the review covers

**Tomorrow's full event list.** Read every event on tomorrow's calendar. Are there any surprises? Anything new that appeared since you last looked?

**Preparation needs.** For each event: what, if anything, do you need to read, prepare, or think about before it? Flag preparation needs now, when you can still do something about them.

**Conflicts.** Are any events overlapping? Are any events placing you in impossible positions (e.g., a commitment in two locations at the same time)?

**Next day first event.** What's the first thing tomorrow? Is there anything you need to do before it? This single question catches the "9am meeting I didn't prepare for" pattern.

**Open loops.** Is there anything that should be on tomorrow's calendar that isn't? Any commitment you made verbally that needs to be formalized?

## The calendar as closure device

The end-of-day review also serves a secondary function: it creates a natural workday endpoint. After the review, you've looked at tomorrow, handled any urgent preparation, and committed to a clear state. The laptop closes with less uncertainty about what's coming.

This is different from the version of evening work where you're checking email "just in case" without any clear endpoint. The review has a defined beginning (open calendar) and end (review complete), which makes it a closure action rather than an open-ended monitoring behavior.

## Implementation

Time: 5 minutes maximum. If it's taking longer, you're either doing more than a review or tomorrow has more complexity than a review can address — in which case the issue is tomorrow's calendar design, not the review process.

Trigger: anchoring this to an existing routine (last coffee of the day, end of last meeting) makes it reliable. Willpower-based habits are less durable.
