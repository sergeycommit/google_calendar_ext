# Calendar Audit: A Structured Method for Evaluating How You Actually Spend Time

*Hacker News post · December 12, 2027*

---

"How do you spend your time?" is a question most people can't answer accurately. Self-reports consistently underestimate meeting time, overestimate focused work time, and miss the invisible overhead (context switching, notification processing, recovery) entirely.

A calendar audit replaces self-report with data.

## The audit methodology

**Phase 1: Data collection (30 min)**

Pull the last 4-6 weeks of Google Calendar data. Google Calendar's search can export data, or you can process it manually. Categorize each event:
- External meetings (client, vendor, investor)
- Internal meetings (syncs, standups, reviews)
- 1-on-1s
- Focus blocks (if you have them)
- Administrative and shallow work
- Personal/health

Aggregate by week: total hours in each category.

**Phase 2: Analysis (20 min)**

Three questions produce the most useful analysis:

**What is your actual meeting load?** (Not what you think it is — what the data shows.) Most knowledge workers underestimate by 25-40%. Seeing the number often produces a visible response.

**What percentage of working hours go to focus work?** For most knowledge workers, this is lower than comfortable. The research benchmark for effective individual contributors is 40-60% focused work; most are at 20-30%.

**What are the biggest discrepancies between planned and actual?** If you plan for focus blocks but they keep getting displaced, that's a pattern. If meetings consistently run longer than scheduled, that's measurable.

**Phase 3: Decision making (10 min)**

For each category that's over-allocated: what specific change would bring it to the desired level?

"Fewer meetings" is not specific. "Reduce internal syncs from 6/week to 4/week by merging [meeting A] and [meeting B] and cancelling [meeting C]" is specific.

## The quarterly cadence

A calendar audit done quarterly shows trends. Meeting load increasing quarter over quarter? Focus time decreasing? These are signals that require structural interventions, not motivational ones.
