Engineering work imposes a specific cost on interruption that most other knowledge work does not. Writing an email, reviewing a document, or making a decision can tolerate a thirty-minute meeting in the middle without significant output loss. Designing a complex system, tracking down a subtle bug, or writing intricate logic cannot — these tasks require sustained mental context that dissipates when attention is pulled away.

The research on this is consistent: it takes most engineers twenty to thirty minutes to fully re-enter a complex coding problem after a significant interruption. A thirty-minute meeting in the middle of a three-hour focus session does not cost thirty minutes — it costs the thirty-minute meeting plus two re-entry periods of twenty minutes each, for a total interruption cost of roughly seventy minutes.

Calendar habits designed around this reality look different from the default knowledge worker setup. The most impactful change is meeting clustering: moving all meetings to two or three designated days and protecting the remaining days for focused engineering work. On meeting days, the context-switching cost is accepted and managed. On focus days, the cost is deliberately avoided.

Within meeting days, the positioning of the daily standup relative to the focus window matters. A standup at 10 AM on a day that starts at 9 AM breaks the best focus window in half. The same standup at 9 AM or at 11:30 AM leaves the peak focus period intact. The time difference is ninety minutes; the output difference is significant over a week and enormous over a month.

For engineers in organizations where meeting culture is meeting-heavy, protecting the calendar requires both individual discipline and clear communication. A brief explanation of why morning focus blocks matter — not just as a preference but as a functional requirement for the type of work engineering requires — produces more cooperation from teammates than silent calendar blocking.
