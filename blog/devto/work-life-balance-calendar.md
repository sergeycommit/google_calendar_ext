Work-life balance is not enforced by intentions. It is enforced by visible structure.

A calendar that does not show personal time treats personal time as infinitely flexible. When a Saturday morning appears empty, it looks available — and work fills available space whenever the week gets busy.

The most effective practice is also the most counterintuitive: block personal time explicitly on the work calendar. Exercise at 7am, family dinner at 6:30pm, 'no calls after 6pm' as a recurring event — these blocks make personal commitments structurally equivalent to meetings. They are visible when someone checks availability before scheduling, and they resist displacement in the same way a confirmed call does.

For remote workers and people with flexible schedules, a consistent shutdown time is the single most effective work-life boundary. A recurring event at the same time each evening — 6pm, 5:30pm, whatever is sustainable — marks the close of the workday. Paired with a 15-minute shutdown ritual (clearing notes, writing tomorrow's first task, closing work applications), it creates a boundary that is both visible and felt.

Weekend protection works the same way: block Saturday and Sunday mornings as Busy. This does not prevent working by choice — it prevents the gradual drift of meetings and commitments into weekend hours without your conscious decision.

Communicate availability proactively: 'I'm available for calls Monday to Friday, 9am to 6pm.' This reduces out-of-hours requests and avoids case-by-case negotiation.
