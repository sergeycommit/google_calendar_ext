There is a specific difference between knowing what time a meeting starts and knowing how many minutes until it starts. The first is information. The second is a decision prompt.

With 23 minutes until a meeting, you can start a task you know you can meaningfully advance in 23 minutes. You can spend five of those minutes writing a quick recap from the previous meeting. You can make a focused decision about how to use the remaining window. Without the countdown — with only '3:30 PM meeting' visible on a static schedule — those 23 minutes are more likely to be consumed by ambient activity, because nothing signals that the window is bounded and therefore valuable.

This is not a theory. People are systematically bad at estimating elapsed time during engaging work. Thirty minutes of interesting work feels like ten. The mind that is fully in a task is not monitoring the clock. This is usually a feature of focus, not a problem — except when a meeting starts at the moment you are deepest into a problem.

A visible countdown externalizes time awareness. It holds the clock while you hold the work. The result is that people who can see time-to-next-event tend to start tasks more quickly, prepare for meetings more reliably, and experience fewer of the disorienting 'oh, that meeting already started' moments that come from losing track of time.

The useful model for the countdown is periodic reference, not constant monitoring. A glance before starting a task tells you what kind of focus is possible — deep versus surface-level. A glance a few minutes before a meeting tells you whether you have time to finish a note. Between back-to-back meetings, a check tells you if the gap is enough to write a brief recap.
