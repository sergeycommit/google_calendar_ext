Google Calendar Goals is a feature that generates either genuine enthusiasm or mild frustration, depending on how you use it. Understanding its actual strengths and limitations determines which outcome you get.

Goals works by automatically scheduling recurring blocks of time for a habit or objective you define — exercise, reading, learning a skill. You set the goal, frequency, and preferred time of day, and the system finds available slots in your calendar and books them. When a conflict arises, it moves the goal session rather than canceling it.

The feature is genuinely useful for one specific profile: personal habits with flexible timing. If it does not matter whether you exercise at 7 AM or noon, only that you exercise three times per week, Goals handles the scheduling friction that often causes these habits to slip. You commit to the goal; the calendar handles when.

The failure modes are predictable. First, overcommitment: setting too many active Goals creates a calendar full of auto-generated blocks that compete with real commitments and each other. Two or three active Goals is a practical maximum for most people. Second, wrong use case: Goals is not well-suited for work-related objectives or any habit that requires specific timing, equipment, a partner, or a particular location. For those, a manually created recurring block gives you the control that Goals deliberately trades away for automation.

The practical approach is to use Goals for personal habits and manual recurring blocks for everything work-related. Protecting morning focus time, for example, works better as a self-created recurring calendar block set to 'busy' than as a Goal — it is precise, visible to others as an occupied slot, and fully under your control.
