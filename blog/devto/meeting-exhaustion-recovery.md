Meeting exhaustion has a specific signature: feeling depleted and unproductive simultaneously. The meetings happened; the focused work did not. The social and cognitive overhead of sustained back-to-back calls, without recovery time between them, produces a kind of tiredness that rest alone does not fix.

Recovery has two phases.

Immediate: a deliberate recovery day with no calls and a lighter task load. Use it for processing — reviewing notes from recent meetings, handling action items, clearing deferred decisions. This is not a vacation; it is the right kind of work (lower cognitive demand, clearing backlog) that prepares the ground for focused output to resume. Jumping immediately from meeting overload to deep work produces frustration, not output.

Structural: after the recovery day, a brief audit of what caused the overload. Which recurring meetings contributed most? Where was the meeting density highest? What was the ratio of meeting time to focused work? The audit produces specific changes — a meeting to reduce, a recurring call to cancel, a policy to establish about available hours or meeting-free mornings.

Rebuilding focus capacity after an extended meeting-heavy period: start with 45-minute sessions before attempting 90-minute ones. The ability to maintain extended concentration often needs gradual rebuilding. These shorter sessions are investments, not compromises.

Meeting exhaustion is a structural signal, not a personal productivity failure. The recovery addresses the acute state; the structural changes address the pattern.
