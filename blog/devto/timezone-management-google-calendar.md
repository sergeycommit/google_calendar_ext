Google Calendar's time zone features are robust enough to handle most distributed team scheduling needs — but they only help when you know they exist and configure them deliberately.

The automatic time zone conversion is the most valuable feature. Events appear in each attendee's local time without any manual calculation. Create a meeting at 10 AM in your time zone, and every attendee sees it at the correct local equivalent. This eliminates one of the most common distributed team errors: everyone looking at the same meeting time but interpreting it as their own local time.

The secondary time zone display adds a reference layer to the day and week view. Enable it in Settings > General > Time zone. You see your primary zone's time labels on the left and the secondary zone's labels on the right. For someone in New York who works closely with a team in Berlin, this makes it immediately visible whether a proposed time falls in business hours for both.

The world clock feature extends this to as many zones as you need. The current time in every zone your team spans is visible in the calendar sidebar. Before creating an invite, a glance at the world clock tells you whether a time is workable for all parties.

The most persistent source of confusion is communication outside the calendar — messages, emails, and verbal references to meeting times without time zone qualifiers. '3 PM' with no zone marker is ambiguous in a distributed context. The habit of always specifying the zone — '3 PM EST' or '15:00 UTC' — eliminates ambiguity at the cost of three characters.
