The standalone calendar app versus Chrome extension comparison is worth examining honestly because the answer is not what productivity enthusiasts often suggest. Dedicated calendar apps like Fantastical are polished, feature-rich, and well-designed. They are also, for most Google Calendar users, more than what the actual daily workflow requires.

The core question is what you actually do with your calendar on a typical day. For most knowledge workers, the answer is: check what is next, join meetings when they start, and occasionally review or schedule things. The heavy planning — weekly reviews, scheduling complex recurring events, managing team calendars — happens infrequently and is well-served by the full Google Calendar interface.

Native apps earn their place in specific scenarios. Multiple calendar providers that need to appear in one view — Google Calendar, Exchange from work, iCloud from a personal Apple account — typically require a native app to manage cleanly. Offline access to calendar data matters for people who travel frequently in low-connectivity situations. Tight macOS integration, including native Spotlight search and menu bar access, is genuinely useful for users deeply in the Apple ecosystem.

For users whose work lives in Chrome and whose primary calendar is Google, these use cases mostly do not apply. A Chrome extension covers the daily workflow — quick schedule checks, meeting joining, countdown awareness — with less friction than launching a separate application and no additional subscription cost for a third-party app.

The tell for whether you need a native app is to spend two weeks tracking what you specifically do with your calendar tool. If you primarily check your schedule and join meetings, the extension use case covers it. If you are creating complex events, managing multiple accounts, and reviewing analytics, a native app earns its place.
