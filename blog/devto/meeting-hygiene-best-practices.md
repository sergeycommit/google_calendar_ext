Most meetings fail before anyone dials in. The failure is not in the meeting itself — it is in the absence of a clear purpose, a real agenda, or any expectation of what will come out of the next 30 or 60 minutes. Meeting hygiene is the collection of habits that fix this before it starts.

The single highest-leverage habit is the agenda. Not a long document — just a few sentences that say what will be discussed and what kind of outcome is expected. A decision? A review? A brainstorm? Naming this changes how people prepare and changes how they behave in the room. Without it, attendees arrive in different mental states and the first ten minutes get spent aligning on what the meeting is even for.

The second habit that separates useful meetings from wasted ones is naming a meeting owner. This is the person responsible not just for scheduling the call but for the outcome. When everyone is responsible, no one is. A named owner handles the agenda, keeps discussion on track, and writes the recap afterward.

Starting on time signals that everyone's schedule is equally important. Waiting for late arrivals is polite to the late person and penalizes everyone who made the effort to be ready. Over time, consistent on-time starts shift team culture without any formal policy.

During the meeting, a live notes document visible to all participants reduces the chance of divergent recollections. When a decision gets made, it goes in the doc immediately. Action items get assigned with names and dates before the meeting ends, not reconstructed from memory afterward.

The recap is where most meeting hygiene fails. People end calls, move to the next thing, and the context dissolves. A three-to-five bullet recap sent within two hours costs maybe five minutes and eliminates most of the confusion that follows. It also serves as a lightweight audit trail for future reference.

For recurring meetings, these habits compound over time. A standing agenda, a shared running notes document, and a consistent format for recaps mean that each meeting builds on the last rather than starting from scratch.

None of this requires special software. It requires intentionality. If you run a meeting, own the prep and own the follow-through. That combination — agenda before, recap after — is the foundation of every meeting worth being in.
