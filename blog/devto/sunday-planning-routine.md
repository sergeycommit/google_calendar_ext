Monday morning is the most reactive moment of many people's workweeks. Two days of email have accumulated. New meeting requests are waiting. The week's shape is unclear. Starting without a plan means the first two hours go to sorting rather than working, and the reactive momentum carries forward through the day.

A Sunday planning routine converts this from a scramble into an intentional start. It does not need to be long. Fifteen to twenty minutes, with a clear structure, is enough to arrive Monday with the week's shape known, priorities set, and any necessary preparation identified.

The structure is simple. Scan the full week's calendar and note the overall density — which days are heavy with meetings, which have open space for focused work. Triage any messages that came in over the weekend without processing them fully — note what needs a response and move on. Name three outcomes you want to produce by Friday; write them down. Block time for those outcomes before Monday and the rest of the week can claim those windows. And identify anything that needs preparation before your first Monday commitment so it can happen before the day starts.

The distinction between setup and work is critical. Sunday planning is for orientation, not output. If you find yourself opening a project or drafting a document during the planning session, the session has drifted into the week. Keep it a review and setup activity — the work itself happens in the week.

Building this as a ritual rather than a task helps. Consistent timing, a comfortable environment, and a short and clear checklist make the session feel like a positive transition into the week rather than an obligation. Most people who do this consistently report significantly less Monday morning stress within two to three weeks.
