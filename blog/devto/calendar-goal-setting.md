The gap between setting a goal and making progress on it is almost always a scheduling problem. The goal exists as an intention — important, agreed upon, worth doing — but it never gets time reserved for it. Meetings fill the calendar. Reactive tasks fill the gaps. The goal waits.

Getting weekly goals onto the calendar is the action that closes the gap between intention and outcome. It is not a planning technique or a mindset shift — it is a mechanical intervention: this work has a time slot, the time slot is protected, the goal advances.

The translation from goal to calendar event requires a bit of specificity. A goal like 'make progress on the product roadmap' does not translate cleanly into a calendar event. A goal like 'Draft: Q3 roadmap priorities for review meeting' does. The event is named by the concrete output expected from that session, which makes both the scheduling decision and the end-of-week review more honest.

Blockade the session with sufficient time. For substantive work — writing, analysis, complex problem-solving — one hour is a minimum for meaningful progress. The slot should be scheduled in your highest-focus hours, not in the late-afternoon gaps that meetings leave over. This is a common mistake: goal work gets the calendar's leftovers rather than its prime real estate.

Deadlines as all-day events add a persistent visual layer. A deadline visible at the top of the day in week view creates a constant signal about how close the due date is relative to the work sessions between now and then. If the deadline is approaching and the work sessions keep getting displaced, the mismatch is visible before it becomes a problem.
