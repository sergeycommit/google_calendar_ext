Recurring meetings decay. This is nearly universal. The first few times a standing meeting runs, participants arrive with fresh context, clear purpose, and genuine engagement. Over weeks and months, preparation drops, agendas blur, and the meeting becomes a habit rather than a tool.

The antidote is a short preparation routine that runs before every recurring call — not just the first few. Five minutes is enough.

Before the meeting: review the notes from last time. What was decided? What was left open? This review surfaces action items that may have slipped and prevents rehashing decisions that were already made. Then identify the one thing you need to accomplish in this meeting — a specific question answered, a blocker resolved, a decision reached. Having a personal goal makes the meeting measurable.

Pull up any materials you plan to reference before the meeting starts. Searching for a document or spreadsheet during the call costs everyone time and attention. Arrive with things ready.

During the meeting, the preparation pays off in two ways. First, you can contribute specific, grounded input rather than general commentary. Second, you can note decisions in real time rather than trying to reconstruct them afterward. Assign names and dates to action items before moving to the next topic — this is where most meeting follow-through breaks down.

After the meeting, spend two minutes updating the running notes document with what was decided and what is next. This single habit eliminates the opening five minutes of the next session, which in recurring meetings is almost always 'so what did we decide last time?'

For recurring meetings that consistently require no preparation because nothing changes between sessions, that is useful information: the meeting may no longer need to exist. A written status update delivered asynchronously would accomplish the same thing with far less calendar overhead.
