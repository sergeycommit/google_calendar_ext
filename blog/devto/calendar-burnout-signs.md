Burnout from a bad calendar has a specific signature. It is not the tiredness of sustained effort — it is the depletion that comes from constant context switching, the dread of a week you have no control over, and the persistent failure to do the work that actually matters.

Seven signs distinguish calendar-driven burnout from general stress.

You feel dread on Sunday evening that is specifically tied to what the calendar holds. When lighter weeks do not produce this feeling, the calendar is the cause.

You regularly end the day without having done the work you intended. Meetings and reactive tasks consumed the hours that were supposed to go elsewhere.

You feel guilty when not in meetings — as if unscheduled time is unproductive time.

You cannot remember the last time you had two uninterrupted hours for focused work.

Your calendar shows back-to-back commitments with no transitions, no lunch, no gaps.

You accept meeting invites by default without evaluating whether your attendance changes the outcome.

You feel resentful rather than energized when focused work finally happens — because it happens late, under pressure, and with insufficient concentration.

The most useful diagnostic: track one week deliberately. For every meeting attended, ask whether your presence changed the outcome. For every displaced focus task, note where it went. At the end of the week, you have a specific list of changes rather than a vague intention to do things differently.
