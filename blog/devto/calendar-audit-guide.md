A calendar audit asks a question most people never get around to: does every recurring commitment on this calendar still deserve to be here? The answer, for most people who look carefully, is no — not for everything.

Meetings from completed projects that no one cancelled. Weekly syncs that outlasted the initiative they were created to support. Optimistic focus blocks that got moved and rescheduled until they live at 6 AM on Fridays. A calendar accumulates this kind of sediment over months and it costs time every single week.

The process is straightforward. Set aside 30-45 minutes. Open your calendar in week or month view. Go through every recurring commitment and ask three questions: what was the original purpose, is that purpose still active, and what breaks if this stops? The answers determine whether the event belongs in the keep, modify, or cut category.

For non-recurring commitments, the question is simpler: is this pulling time toward or away from the work that matters most this quarter? Some things land in the wrong slot even if they are individually valuable — a worthwhile professional development commitment scheduled at the wrong time of day, blocking peak focus hours that would serve your most important project better.

The modify category is where interesting work happens. A 60-minute weekly meeting that could be 30. A daily check-in that would work better as a bi-weekly one. A solo planning block that got scheduled too early in the week before other commitments clarify the priorities. Many calendar problems are not 'this should not exist' — they are 'this needs a different shape.'

Act on the audit results quickly. The keep list stays as is. The cut list gets cancelled with short, honest messages to any affected parties. The modify list gets rescheduled or reformatted. Then add what the audit reveals is missing — focus blocks, preparation time, buffer between meetings.
