Most people use Google Calendar to record meetings. A smaller group use it to plan their entire day. Time blocking is the habit that separates these two groups.

The idea is simple: instead of a to-do list you return to whenever time appears, you assign each task a specific calendar slot. A deep work session gets a 90-minute block at 9am. Email gets a 30-minute batch at 4pm. Each block has a name specific enough to answer the question: what exactly happens here?

This works because calendar events carry weight. A meeting at 2pm and a focus block at 10am look the same on the grid. When something is on the calendar, it is real. When it lives only on a task list, it is optional.

The most common mistakes: blocking 100% of your hours (one disruption cascades through the whole day), using vague names like 'Work' (forces recall every time you see it), and leaving blocks set to Free (meeting invites land inside them).

A more resilient setup: block 60–70% of your available time. Mark focus blocks as Busy. Give every block a specific task name. Review what drifted at the end of each week and adjust.
