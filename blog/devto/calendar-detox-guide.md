A calendar detox is not about deleting everything and starting fresh. It is a structured audit-and-rebuild — removing what has accumulated without clear purpose, and adding back the structure that was missing.

The process has four steps.

Step one: the audit. List every recurring meeting. For each one: does it have a current purpose? Is my attendance required? Is the frequency right? Mark each as Keep, Reduce, Convert to async, or Cancel. Do not change anything yet — just make the list. Most people find 20–30% of recurring meetings marked for change.

Step two: execute. Work through the list. Cancel or decline the meetings marked for removal — a brief message works: 'I've been reviewing my recurring commitments and removing this one; happy to reconnect if something specific comes up.' Reduce frequencies. Propose async alternatives. This takes one to two weeks because it involves other people's calendars.

Step three: rebuild with structure. After removing weight, add the structure that was missing: a morning focus block, a shallow work batch window, a weekly planning ritual. Add one thing per week. An overcrowded rebuilt calendar is not better than the one you just cleaned.

Step four: maintenance. A monthly 15-minute scan of recurring meetings prevents re-accumulation. A filter on incoming invites — does my attendance change the outcome? — prevents the gradual drift back toward overload.

The most common mistake: waiting until burnout is severe before starting. The detox takes one hour to plan and produces relief within two weeks.
