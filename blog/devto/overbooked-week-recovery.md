An overbooked week is manageable with the right approach. The goal is not to execute it perfectly — it is to get through it without making commitments you will regret, and to ensure next week is not a repeat.

Rule one: do not add to it. The instinct to say yes to one more thing during an already overloaded week turns a recoverable situation into a breaking one. New commitments during an overbooked week have no room to land.

Rule two: triage the week. On Sunday evening or Monday morning, review what is scheduled and ask: which meetings truly require my participation, and which could be handled by a note or summary? Which tasks have genuine deadlines this week, and which can slide? The triage identifies what cannot move; everything else becomes a deliberate decision rather than a default obligation.

Rule three: protect one anchor block. Even in the heaviest week, a 60-minute protected window for one task that matters changes the psychological character of the week. It creates at least one moment of intentional work in a week that otherwise feels entirely reactive.

Rule four: end the week deliberately. A 15-minute Friday review — what moved, what the backlog is, what needs to happen next week — prevents the overloaded week from becoming invisible to the week that follows.

Rule five: set up the recovery week before this one ends. Block additional focus time for next week before it fills. Decline anything added this week that does not belong in next week.
