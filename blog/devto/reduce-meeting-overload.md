Meeting overload does not happen because people love meetings. It happens because meetings are the path of least resistance when communication feels uncertain. Unclear whether an email will get read? Schedule a meeting. Not sure if everyone is aligned? Schedule a meeting. Over time, these individual decisions stack into a calendar that leaves no room for the work the meetings are supposed to produce.

The sustainable fix is not willpower or learned aggression toward invites. It is changing the defaults that generate unnecessary meetings in the first place.

The most effective default change is async first. Any communication that is primarily informational — status updates, progress reports, announcements — should not occupy a live slot on five people's calendars. A written update, a shared document, or a recorded walkthrough communicates the same content with more flexibility and no scheduling coordination.

For the meetings that do need to exist, shortening the default duration creates automatic focus. A 25-minute meeting accomplishes most of what a 30-minute meeting does, and the five-minute gap gives people time to reach the next call without being late. At scale, this alone reduces the back-to-back pressure that makes meeting-heavy days feel exhausting.

Another underused tool: require an agenda before confirming attendance. This has two effects. It filters out low-clarity meetings — organizers who cannot write a sentence about what the meeting is for often realize they do not need the meeting. It also improves the meetings that do happen, because both parties arrive with shared expectations.

Calendar blocking is the visibility layer that makes all of this work. When your calendar shows only meetings, people treat every open slot as available. When focus time, lunch, and transition time are blocked, your actual availability is honest and visible. Fewer spontaneous invitations land in those windows.

For recurring meetings, a quarterly audit pays consistent dividends. Pull up every recurring meeting on your calendar and ask: what would happen if this stopped? If the answer is nothing, or if the last few instances could have been handled with a written update, that meeting is a candidate for cancellation.
