Hybrid work schedules introduce a calendar complexity that office-only or remote-only schedules do not have: your physical location matters for your availability, and that information needs to be visible to the people who schedule with you.

A meeting that requires in-person attendance cannot meaningfully happen on a remote day. A spontaneous whiteboard session is only possible when you are physically in the office. Conversely, a long focused writing session is often harder in the office than at home, where ambient office interruptions do not exist. Matching work type to location — and making that matching visible — is the fundamental challenge of hybrid calendar management.

Google Calendar's working location setting is the most direct tool for this. Setting 'Office' or 'Home' for each day makes your location visible to anyone who views your calendar without requiring a daily Slack announcement or a separate shared document. Colleagues scheduling with you can see at a glance whether the day you are considering for an in-person meeting is an office day.

The structural discipline of clustering in-person work on office days and focused work on remote days prevents the worst hybrid outcome: office days that are just video-call days with commute overhead. Office days should earn their commute through collaboration and spontaneous conversations that remote work genuinely cannot replicate. Remote days should earn their flexibility through focused, uninterrupted work.

Protecting at least one morning of deep work on each office day takes explicit calendar blocking. Office environments generate spontaneous conversations, drop-by questions, and ad-hoc meetings that erode focused time faster than a remote environment does. Without a blocked focus period, office days can end with the realization that you did nothing that could not have been done at home.
