Calendar boundaries with coworkers are not about being unavailable. They are about being predictably available during defined windows and protected during others — which is more useful to colleagues than infinite, ambiguous availability.

Most colleagues prefer to know when you are free and when you are not. Ambiguous availability generates friction: they schedule at random times, you decline or reschedule, and both parties waste effort. A clear window — 'available for calls 10am to 4pm' — makes scheduling straightforward.

The least confrontational boundary is a well-structured calendar. Focus blocks marked Busy show as unavailable when someone checks before scheduling. This communicates 'this time is committed' without a verbal boundary, and it works for the majority of colleagues who check availability before scheduling.

For those who do not check, a proactive availability statement is more effective than reactive defense. A brief message to frequent collaborators — 'I protect mornings for focused work and am available for calls from 10am' — sets the expectation before requests arrive. Proactive communication requires one conversation; reactive defense requires many.

When a meeting does land over a focus block, the cleanest response is brief and direct: 'I have a focus commitment at that time — can we move to [alternative slot]?' Apply this consistently and without apology. Consistent defense teaches that the boundary is real; inconsistency teaches that it is negotiable.
