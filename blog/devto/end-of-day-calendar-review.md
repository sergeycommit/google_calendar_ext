The five-minute end-of-day review is one of those habits that seems too small to matter until you do it consistently for a month. The difference in how mornings feel — with and without it — is not subtle.

The review serves three functions: closing out today, preparing tomorrow, and releasing the mental load of open items. The third function is the one people underestimate. When unfinished tasks and unresolved questions remain in working memory, they create a background anxiety that persists into the evening and reactivates in the morning. Writing them down — even a short, imperfect list — releases the mental hold. The tasks have not disappeared, but they are now in a system rather than in your head.

The structure of the review is simple. Scan tomorrow's calendar: know every event, its time, and what preparation is needed. Write down any follow-ups from today's meetings that might dissolve overnight. Identify the first thing to do tomorrow morning — not a long list, just the one most important thing. Note any tasks that did not get done and either reschedule them consciously or deprioritize them.

The whole process takes five minutes. If it is taking fifteen, something has expanded beyond the review's purpose. Planning and decision-making belong in a weekly planning ritual (see our guide on building a weekly planning ritual), not in the daily closing review.

Consistency is what makes the habit valuable. A daily review done every working day changes the quality of mornings in a measurable way. An occasional review provides a fraction of that benefit. Like most habits, the return is in the consistency, not the execution quality on any individual day.
