The mobile Google Calendar app is optimized for engagement, which is a slightly different goal from being optimized for efficiency. The infinite scroll, the visual calendar, and the easy navigation are designed to keep you in the app. For most routine calendar checks, you want the opposite: get the information you need in the shortest possible time and get back to what you were doing.

The first improvement that changes mobile calendar behavior is a home screen widget. Both iOS and Android support Google Calendar widgets that display upcoming events without opening the app. A glance at the home screen answers the most common calendar question — what is next and when — in about two seconds. This eliminates most of the proactive app-opening that turns into unintended browsing.

If you do open the app, setting schedule view as your default view is more efficient than month or week view. Schedule view shows events as a chronological list, skipping empty days, so you see only the relevant upcoming commitments without scrolling through empty grid space.

Notifications reduce the need for proactive checking. If you set a fifteen-minute notification for meetings you might otherwise forget, you eliminate the 'better check if I have anything coming up' app opens that happen every thirty minutes. Good notification configuration means you only open the calendar when you want to, not because you are worried you might be missing something.

For actual planning work — weekly reviews, creating recurring events, managing complex scheduling — the desktop version of Google Calendar is better suited. Mobile handles the quick checks and on-the-go creation; desktop handles the structured planning.
