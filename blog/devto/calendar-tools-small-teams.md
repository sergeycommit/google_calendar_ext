Small teams have a genuine scheduling advantage over large organizations: they can establish norms quickly and do not need complex software to coordinate. The right calendar tool is almost always the simplest one that covers the actual needs.

For most small teams, that is Google Calendar's free tier. Shared team calendars, individual calendars with visibility settings, and Google Meet integration handle the coordination needs of teams under 20 people without additional tools or subscription costs.

The setup is straightforward: a shared team calendar for company-wide events and deadlines, individual calendars shared with view access so anyone can check availability before scheduling, and a few team norms — default availability hours, how to mark focus blocks, meeting-free windows.

A scheduling link tool (Calendly, SavvyCal) adds value when external booking is frequent. Client calls, sales calls, interviews — letting external parties book directly into available slots eliminates email back-and-forth. For internal scheduling, direct calendar checking is sufficient.

What to avoid: enterprise calendar management tools designed for 500-person organizations. The setup cost, learning curve, and maintenance burden far exceed the coordination value they provide at small team scale.

The complete stack for most small teams: Google Calendar (free), one scheduling link tool for external booking, and optionally one Chrome extension per member for individual quick access. Core functionality costs nothing.
