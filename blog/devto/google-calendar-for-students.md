The first week of a semester is the most important calendar week of the academic term. The decisions made about what goes on the calendar — and how it is structured — determine how well the rest of the semester is managed. Students who spend an hour in week one entering all deadlines, exam dates, and class times from every syllabus start the semester with complete visibility. Students who add things as they remember them spend the semester in a mild state of scheduling uncertainty.

The course-per-calendar structure is the foundation. Create a separate Google Calendar for each course, assign each a distinct color, and use those calendars consistently throughout the semester. In week view, the color distribution shows at a glance which courses are demanding the most calendar attention this week and whether any course has an upcoming deadline that is underrepresented in the schedule.

Front-loading syllabus deadlines is the single highest-leverage action. Pull the syllabus for every course in the first week and add every assignment due date, exam date, and project milestone to the appropriate calendar. Add them as all-day events on the due date. This takes approximately one hour total across all courses and eliminates the 'I forgot that was due this week' experience for the rest of the semester.

Exams require backward planning. Adding the exam date is necessary but not sufficient. Working backward from the exam to block study sessions in the preceding days creates the plan rather than just the deadline. A Friday exam with no study blocks earlier in the week is a hope, not a schedule.

A complete calendar — one that includes personal commitments, work shifts, and social activities alongside academic requirements — gives an honest picture of available study time. A student calendar with only academic events can look manageable when three job shifts and a weekly commitment have not been added yet.
