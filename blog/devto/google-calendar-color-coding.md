Google Calendar gives you eleven colors for events. Most people who try to build a color system use too many of them, create a system they cannot maintain without conscious effort, and abandon it within a week.

A working color system does one thing: lets you scan the week view and understand the shape of your commitments without reading a single event title. That requires a maximum of four categories, each with a clear and consistent meaning.

The most useful starting framework: red for things that cannot move — hard deadlines, high-stakes commitments, or events you are responsible for running. Blue for external meetings — anything involving clients, partners, or people outside your immediate team. Green for focus time — blocks you protect for deep, uninterrupted work. Gray for low-priority items — reminders, optional events, personal errands.

Applying this at scale is easier than it sounds. Google Calendar allows you to color-code entire calendars, so your Work calendar gets one default color and your Personal calendar gets another. You only override the calendar default for individual events that need a specific signal — a client call in a work calendar gets blue; a hard deadline gets red. Everything else inherits the calendar color automatically.

The system earns its value through consistency. Once the patterns are established, your weekly view becomes a visual map: red blocks tell you where the pressure is, green blocks show you where real work happens, and the absence of green on a busy week is a warning signal before the week starts.

Some practical rules to keep the system working over time: never use a color for a one-off reason. If a specific event does not fit a category, leave it at the calendar default rather than inventing a new meaning for an unused color. Fewer categories with perfect consistency always beats a comprehensive system applied loosely.
