Creative work has a warm-up cost that most non-designers underestimate. Before a designer reaches the state of mind where complex layout decisions, visual problem-solving, and design system thinking genuinely flows, fifteen to twenty minutes of ramp-up is required. An interruption resets this cost entirely.

This means a design day fragmented by meetings does not produce a proportionally reduced amount of good work — it produces work that is systematically shallower than what longer blocks would enable. The meeting in the middle of the morning does not cost thirty minutes; it costs the morning.

Protecting creative flow requires two things from the calendar: long uninterrupted blocks in the morning (when creative energy is highest for most designers) and deliberate clustering of meetings and reviews to specific days or time windows. The combination creates design days and meeting days, rather than a week of fragmented half-days that serve neither purpose well.

Design reviews deserve special consideration. They are not standard meetings — they require prepared work, emotional resilience for feedback, and processing time afterward. Grouping reviews together on designated days contains the preparation and recovery cost. Placing reviews in the afternoon rather than the morning preserves the best creative hours for creation rather than presentation.

Async feedback channels — Figma comments, shared document annotations, recorded walkthroughs — handle a significant portion of design feedback without requiring synchronous time. Training collaborators to use these channels during focus hours and reserve synchronous reviews for complex or sensitive feedback reduces the meeting load without reducing the feedback quality.
