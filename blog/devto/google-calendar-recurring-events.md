Recurring events in Google Calendar are one of those features where the basic functionality is obvious and the useful nuances are hidden until you need them and cannot find them.

The most important concept to understand is the edit scope. Every time you edit a recurring event, Google Calendar asks which events you want to modify: just this one, this and all following, or the entire series. Getting this wrong — choosing 'This event' when you meant 'All events,' or vice versa — leads to a calendar full of mismatched instances that become very difficult to untangle.

A practical rule: when a change is permanent — the time moved, the meeting room changed, the agenda evolved — edit 'All events.' When something changes for one week only, edit 'This event.' The 'This and following events' option is most useful after a schedule disruption where the recurrence shifts permanently from a certain date forward.

One tip most users miss: set an end date when you create a recurring event. Open-ended series accumulate in your calendar forever. A quarterly review series created in January that runs without an end date will still be on your calendar in three years, even if the review cycle changed in June. Giving a series an end date forces a periodic review of whether it should continue.

Custom recurrence patterns are worth knowing about. The standard options — daily, weekly, monthly — cover most needs, but the 'Custom' option allows every two weeks, the third Tuesday of the month, or the last Friday of the quarter. For events that follow business planning cycles, custom patterns match the reality of your schedule rather than approximating it.
