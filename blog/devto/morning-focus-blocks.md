The first hours of the day are often the most cognitively available. Most people spend them on email.

Morning focus blocks fix this by claiming the peak concentration window before meetings, messages, and reactive demands take over. The mechanics are simple: a recurring calendar event, marked Busy, at the start of your workday, dedicated to work that requires sustained attention.

The reason this matters: concentration quality is not uniform across the day. Willpower, decision capacity, and the ability to hold complex ideas in working memory all tend to decrease as cognitive load accumulates. For most people, early morning is the highest-quality working time available. Left unprotected, it fills with reactive tasks that can happen just as well at 3pm.

Common mistakes when setting up morning blocks: making the start time too early to sustain, naming the block generically ('Focus time' instead of the specific project), leaving it as Free so it accepts meeting invites, and using the block for email instead of deep work.

A more durable setup: create a recurring block before your first regular meeting. Name it after your current major project. Set it to Busy. Start with 60–90 minutes — long enough for meaningful output, short enough to maintain every day. Use it only for cognitively demanding work that benefits from uninterrupted attention.

The block does not need to be perfect. It needs to exist consistently. Even a reliable 60-minute window each morning compounds into significant output over a month.
