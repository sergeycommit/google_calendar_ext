Calendar transparency is one of the few workplace topics where the answer genuinely depends on context rather than yielding to a universal rule. Too little transparency creates coordination friction — the endless 'are you free?' messages and scheduling back-and-forth that distributed teams often suffer. Too much transparency exposes content that colleagues do not need to see and that the calendar owner may reasonably want to keep private.

The most useful framework is tiered: different sharing levels for different relationships. Direct teammates who coordinate work with you daily benefit from full event-detail sharing — they can see what you are working on, anticipate when you are in a heads-down session, and schedule around your existing commitments without needing to ask. The coordination value of this transparency is high and the privacy cost is low for work calendar content.

For the broader organization — colleagues you collaborate with occasionally but not daily — free/busy sharing provides the scheduling information they need (is this person available?) without the content details they do not need (what meetings are they in?). This is the appropriate default for organizational calendar sharing.

Personal events are the complicating factor. A work calendar that shows the medical appointment, the school pickup, and the afternoon personal errand is oversharing for most workplace contexts. The solution is not to omit these from the calendar — doing so leaves the time looking available when it is not — but to put them on a separate personal calendar with restricted sharing. The work calendar gets a generic busy block at the affected time; the personal calendar has the details for your own reference.

The question of content versus availability is useful for calibrating where on the transparency spectrum to sit. Most scheduling coordination needs availability information: is this person free at 3 PM? Full event detail sharing provides this plus context. Free/busy provides only availability. For most remote team scheduling, availability is what matters, and the additional context from full sharing is a bonus rather than a requirement.
