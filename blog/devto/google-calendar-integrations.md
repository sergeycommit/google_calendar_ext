Google Calendar integrations range from genuinely time-saving to new sources of maintenance overhead that cost more than they save. The difference is specificity: an integration that solves a specific, recurring friction point in your actual workflow tends to become invisible in the best way — it just works. An integration that solves a theoretical problem you rarely encounter tends to sit unused while still requiring periodic updates and permissions management.

The category with the clearest return on investment is scheduling tools. If you regularly coordinate meetings with people outside your organization, a tool like Calendly that connects to your Google Calendar and lets others book available slots directly eliminates significant email back-and-forth. The integration works in the background; the benefit appears every time a meeting gets booked without a chain of 'what about Tuesday?' emails.

The Slack integration is similarly low-maintenance and consistently useful for teams. Once connected, Google Calendar automatically updates your Slack status when you are in a meeting, based on current calendar events. Teammates see the calendar icon and know you are unavailable without you needing to manually set your status before each call.

Conference tool integrations — Google Meet and Zoom generating automatic meeting links in calendar events — eliminate a small but annoying manual step that previously required copying a link into every event description. Enabling automatic Meet link creation in Settings makes this seamless.

For freelancers and anyone who bills by time, connecting a time tracking tool like Toggl or Harvest to Google Calendar can pre-populate time entries based on calendar events, reducing manual logging.

The caution flag goes up for AI scheduling assistants and task manager integrations. Both categories have real use cases but both can create calendar noise or unexpected behavior that requires active management.
