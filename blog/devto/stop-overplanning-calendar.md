Overplanning is a real productivity trap. Not because planning is bad, but because a calendar planned too tightly has no room for the unexpected — and the unexpected always happens.

The pattern: scheduling every hour with precision, creating a plan that works only if nothing changes, feeling frustrated when it deviates, and spending Sunday evenings on detailed weekly planning that will be obsolete by Tuesday morning.

The characteristic failure: a day that looks perfect at 8am and is already broken by 9:30. Not because you planned badly. Because detailed plans are fragile.

A functional calendar for most knowledge workers needs only: meetings that are already scheduled, one or two daily focus blocks for the most important work, and visible white space for the unexpected. This skeleton provides orientation without constraining every hour.

Simplifying an over-complicated calendar is quick: remove any recurring block whose removal would not actually change how the week goes. Eliminate color categories you cannot identify without looking at the legend. Remove events that track activities that do not need calendar slots.

What remains is the minimal calendar. It takes almost no maintenance, it provides the structure needed to start each day with clear direction, and it survives the unexpected without collapsing.

The test for any planning system: does it reduce the number of decisions you need to make, or does it add to them? A good calendar system answers 'what do I do when I sit down to work?' An overplanned one answers that question and creates ten more.
