The reason most knowledge workers have less focused work time than they need is not that they lack discipline — it is that open calendar space gets claimed by default. Meeting organizers look for available windows, and available means unblocked. Focus time that is not on the calendar is available.

The fix is mechanical: put focus time on the calendar before meetings can claim it. Create a recurring block during your peak cognitive hours — for most people, mid-morning. Mark it as busy. Give it a specific name so its purpose is clear. It now occupies that window on your schedule the way a meeting does, and meeting organizers see it as unavailable.

The timing decision is the highest-leverage part of this. Many people block focus time in the late afternoon or at whatever hours meetings have not already claimed. This produces low-quality focused work in a low-energy window — not because the hours are blocked, but because the best hours went to meetings. Reversing the priority — scheduling focus time first in the best hours, then filling meeting requests around that — produces demonstrably better output.

For Google Workspace users, the Focus Time event type adds an automatic layer: it can activate Do Not Disturb in Google Chat during the block, reducing the interruption rate without requiring manual toggling.

Durability requires maintenance. Focus blocks erode over time the same way meeting-free days do — through individually justifiable exceptions that accumulate into a pattern. A monthly check on how often your focus blocks ran as planned is the lightest-touch maintenance process that catches this drift early.
