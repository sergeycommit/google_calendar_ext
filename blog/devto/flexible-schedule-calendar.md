Flexible work schedules create one challenge that rigid schedules do not: the schedule is invisible to colleagues by default. When everyone works 9-to-5, the shared rhythm provides implicit coordination. When schedules vary — a 7-to-3 arrangement, a four-day week, staggered start times — the coordination that the shared rhythm provided needs to be made explicit somewhere. The calendar is the right place.

Setting working hours in Google Calendar to your actual schedule — not the standard hours, but your real hours — is the first step. This creates the visible structure that a flexible schedule needs. Colleagues scheduling with you see a warning when they try to book outside your working window. The warning does not prevent invites, but it shifts the social default: scheduling at 4 PM for someone who ends at 3 is now a visible choice, not an invisible assumption.

For non-working days, a full-day blocked event or the working hours setting's 'no working hours' option for that day creates the same visibility. Your day off is not a mystery gap in your schedule — it is a clearly marked unavailable day that people can see before scheduling.

The overlap window — the hours when your flexible schedule aligns with your colleagues' standard schedules — is the key coordination concept. Identifying it and communicating it explicitly ('I'm available for calls from 9 AM to noon') gives colleagues a bounded window for synchronous requests and makes the rest of your schedule clearly asynchronous territory.

Flexible schedules erode through exceptions. The 7-to-3 arrangement that accommodates one 4 PM meeting for a good reason becomes a 7-to-3:30 arrangement, then 7-to-4. The same enforcement mechanisms that protect meeting-free days and work-from-home boundaries apply here: explicit calendar blocks at the boundaries, consistent enforcement, and periodic review of whether the schedule is holding.
