A meeting-heavy calendar does not make time blocking impossible. It makes it necessary.

The instinct in a dense schedule is to put focused work in the gaps between meetings. The problem: a 45-minute gap between two calls is often 30 minutes of real usable time after mental recovery and meeting prep. Most cognitively demanding tasks need more than 30 uninterrupted minutes to produce meaningful output.

The most effective first step is not adding blocks — it is auditing existing meetings. For each recurring call: if this meeting were cancelled, what would actually break? If the answer is 'nothing immediately,' it is a candidate for removal or conversion to an async update. One removed meeting creates a reliable window that no amount of gap-filling can match.

After the audit, place focus time where it is most protected. A morning anchor block — before the first meeting of the day — gives you uninterrupted access to your best hours. A meeting-free half-day (one morning or afternoon per week with no calls) is the single most impactful change for people with dense schedules.

Defend the blocks you create by marking them Busy, communicating your availability policy to frequent collaborators, and holding the boundary when meeting invites arrive. 'My 9–10:30am is for deep work' is a policy most colleagues will respect once they understand it is consistent.
