The decision to schedule meetings back-to-back is made quickly and costs slowly. The immediate logic is clean: the calendar is full, there is a lot to accomplish, and leaving gaps feels wasteful. The actual cost reveals itself across the day: people arriving late, still mentally in the previous call; decisions made with less care than they deserve; follow-up notes never written because the next meeting started before the last one fully ended.

Buffer time between meetings is not a luxury. It is the minimum condition for attending meetings effectively rather than merely attending them.

Five minutes is enough for the basics — closing one mental context before opening the next, navigating to the correct link, and arriving without the breathless energy of someone who just ran from another call. Ten minutes allows you to jot down the key decisions from the previous meeting before memory decays, drink water, and look at the next meeting's agenda. Fifteen minutes allows genuine reset after something emotionally demanding.

The most practical way to build buffer time is to change your meeting duration defaults. Most calendar tools allow you to set a default meeting length. If yours is 30 minutes, change it to 25. If it is 60, change it to 50. This creates gaps automatically without requiring negotiation on every invite. The five or ten minutes appear because the meeting ends before the next one begins.

A secondary technique for meetings you know will be complex or emotionally loaded: block a short transition event afterward. Mark it as busy. The calendar shows it and people will not schedule into it. You do not need to explain it.

Teams where back-to-back scheduling is the norm are signaling something about how they view meetings — as the primary work rather than a tool for enabling work. Protecting transition time is a small act with an outsize effect on how everyone experiences the day.
