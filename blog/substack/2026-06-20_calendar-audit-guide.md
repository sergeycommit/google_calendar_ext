# I Spent 2 Hours Auditing My Calendar. Here's What I Learned.

*Schedule Calendar Weekly · December 11, 2026*

---

A calendar audit is one of those things that sounds faintly administrative but produces genuine insight. You look at where your time actually went — not where you planned for it to go, not where you intended it to go — and the gap between the plan and the reality is usually instructive.

I did my first real audit at the end of a quarter when I felt like I'd worked hard but couldn't account for where the work went. The audit answered that question immediately.

## What a calendar audit reveals

**Meeting load.** How many hours per week, on average, were in meetings? How has that changed over the quarter? Is it trending up, down, or stable?

**Deep work ratio.** What percentage of your available work hours went to focused individual work vs. meetings vs. admin?

**Category distribution.** Of your scheduled blocks, which categories were they in? If you use color coding, this is a 10-second visual scan.

**Recurring vs. ad-hoc.** What portion of your week was planned recurring structure vs. reactive scheduling? A calendar heavy on ad-hoc events suggests you're operating in reactive mode.

## The questions that surface the real issues

After gathering the numbers, three questions produce the most useful reflection:

1. **Where is the biggest gap between intention and reality?** You planned deep work mornings; were they actually used for deep work?

2. **What took more time than you thought?** Usually a category or person or project that didn't feel expensive but was.

3. **What would you remove if you could remove anything?** Sometimes the answer reveals something you have the power to change immediately.

## How often to do this

Quarterly is enough for most people. The goal isn't constant monitoring — it's periodic reality checks that catch drift before it becomes the new normal.

> The calendar is an honest witness. It doesn't remember your intentions — only what you actually scheduled. Reading it carefully tells you more about your priorities in practice than any goal-setting exercise.

---

**This week's takeaway:**
- A calendar audit reveals actual time allocation vs. intended time allocation — the gap is the insight
- Review meeting load, deep work ratio, and category distribution at minimum
- Quarterly audits catch drift before it becomes entrenched

*End of quarter: the right time for this. Set aside 90 minutes and look honestly at the last three months.*
