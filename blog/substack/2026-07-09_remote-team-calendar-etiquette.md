# The Unwritten Calendar Rules That Remote Teams Need to Write Down

*Schedule Calendar Weekly · April 23, 2027*

---

In an office, many calendar norms are enforced by environment. You can see that someone is on a call. You can tell when the conference room is occupied. The physical signals of "I'm busy" don't require a calendar policy.

Remote teams don't have these signals. What's visible is only what's on the calendar — and most teams have never agreed on what should be there, how it should be labeled, or what the calendar actually means.

The result: meeting invites that arrive without warning, scheduling across time zones that nobody finds convenient, and colleagues who assume "no calendar event = available" even during deep work blocks.

## The norms worth making explicit

**Working hours.** Agree on the team's shared window — not to require everyone to be available for all of it, but to define when meetings can reasonably be scheduled. "We schedule during 9am–5pm Pacific" is a norm. "Meetings can happen any time in anyone's day" is chaos.

**Calendar visibility.** Agree that everyone makes their calendar visible to the team (at minimum free/busy). Scheduling without this information is guesswork.

**What a block means.** Does a time block mean "do not schedule me here" or "I'd prefer not to be scheduled here"? The difference matters. Agree on it.

**Response time to invites.** When someone gets an invite, how quickly should they accept, decline, or propose an alternative? 24 hours is a reasonable default.

**Deep work visibility.** If people time-block, do they share that with the team? Seeing that a colleague has a writing block from 9–11am changes how you interpret their unavailability.

## The conversation to have

These norms don't require a policy document. A 30-minute team discussion that produces five written agreements is enough. The writing isn't bureaucracy — it's the difference between a norm that exists in one person's head and a norm that the whole team shares.

> The cost of having this conversation once is far lower than the cost of recurring misunderstandings that pile up over months.

---

**This week's takeaway:**
- Remote teams need explicit calendar agreements because the physical signals that enforce office norms don't exist
- The essentials: shared working window, mutual calendar visibility, agreed meaning of calendar blocks
- A 30-minute conversation and five written agreements prevents months of scheduling friction
