# The Calendar Trick That Keeps Meetings From Eating Your Week

*Schedule Calendar Weekly · April 24, 2026*

---

If you've never audited a week of your meetings, I'd encourage you to do it just once. Open your calendar, look at the last five days, and add up the hours. For a lot of people, the number is somewhere between alarming and depressing.

Meetings aren't going away. But the way they land on a calendar — scattered, back-to-back, at all hours — doesn't have to be the default.

## The problem with a meeting-only calendar

When meetings are the only intentional items on your calendar, the implicit message is that everything else happens in the gaps. You check email between calls. You try to write in 25-minute windows. You context-switch constantly and wonder why the actual work never moves fast.

The gaps between meetings aren't a work schedule. They're leftover time. And leftover time is the worst environment for anything requiring real attention.

## Using blocks to give work the same status as meetings

The shift that matters: treat deep work with the same calendar weight as a meeting. A 90-minute writing block on Tuesday at 9am isn't "time I hope to use." It's an appointment I've made with myself, marked Busy, given a specific name.

When meetings try to fill that slot, they see a conflict. Not an impenetrable wall — you can still move things — but a signal that something else was already claimed.

## Designing a meeting-safe week

A few structural approaches that help:

**Meeting clusters.** Group meetings into specific days or half-days. Two or three meeting-heavy days can protect the rest of the week for concentrated work. This requires some negotiation and a willingness to push back on "can you do Tuesday at 10?" — but it compounds quickly.

**Meeting-free mornings.** Protect your first 2–3 hours for work blocks. No meetings before a certain time. Even two or three days a week with a protected morning makes a measurable difference.

**Time limits per day.** Decide in advance how many hours of meetings are acceptable on any given day. When you hit the limit, the answer to new meeting requests is "next week."

> The hardest part isn't the tactics. It's accepting that protecting your work time is a legitimate professional act, not a failure to be a team player.

**This week's takeaway:**
- Meetings and deep work compete for the same hours — only one gets calendar slots by default
- Treat work blocks with the same seriousness as meetings: specific names, Busy status
- Clustering meetings into certain days protects the rest of the week structurally
