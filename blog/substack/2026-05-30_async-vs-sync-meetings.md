# Before You Schedule That Meeting, Ask Yourself This

*Schedule Calendar Weekly · July 17, 2026*

---

The default response to "we need to align on this" is to schedule a meeting. It's what organizations are trained to do. The problem is that meetings are synchronous — they require everyone to be available at the same time, in the same mental state, focused on the same thing.

That's a high coordination cost. And for many conversations, it's unnecessary.

## The question to ask first

Before scheduling a meeting, ask: does this require real-time interaction?

Real-time interaction is genuinely needed for: brainstorming that builds on itself in the moment, emotionally charged conversations where tone matters, live decision-making under time pressure, and collaborative tasks where two people working simultaneously produces something neither could alone.

Real-time interaction is *not* needed for: status updates, information sharing, progress reports, context-setting, or anything where the communication can be asynchronous without loss of quality.

Most calendar invites are responses to one of these scenarios. Only the first list justifies a synchronous meeting.

## What async actually looks like

Asynchronous communication doesn't mean slow communication. A well-written Loom video, a structured Notion page, a clear Slack message with explicit questions — these can achieve what a 30-minute meeting was going to achieve, without requiring four people to be free at the same time.

The keys to async that works:

**Be complete.** An async message that prompts a back-and-forth clarification chain is worse than a meeting. Write the full context, the specific questions, and the decision needed.

**Set a response window.** "I need a decision on this by Thursday EOD" gives people a deadline without requiring synchronous alignment.

**Document outcomes.** Whatever is decided asynchronously goes into the shared record immediately. No meeting notes to write; the conversation *is* the record.

## When synchronous is still right

The list above isn't an argument against meetings. Some problems genuinely need the energy of real-time collaboration. Difficult interpersonal conversations should be synchronous. Creative sprints benefit from being in the room together. Relationship-building happens face-to-face.

> The goal isn't fewer meetings for their own sake. It's the right meetings for the things that require them, and async for everything else. Most teams have the ratio backwards.

---

**This week's takeaway:**
- Ask "does this require real-time interaction?" before every meeting invite you send
- Status updates, information sharing, and progress reports rarely justify synchronous time
- Async works when the message is complete, the deadline is clear, and the outcome is documented
