# Protecting Focus Time Across a Distributed Team Is Harder Than It Sounds

*Schedule Calendar Weekly · June 25, 2027*

---

On a co-located team, protecting focus time is an individual problem. You close your door, put on headphones, and people can see you're working. On a distributed team, it's a collective problem. There's no visible signal that someone is in deep focus. There's no social norm enforced by proximity. Every "quick question" on Slack or surprise meeting invite arrives with the same perceived urgency.

Individual calendar blocks help. Team norms help more.

## Why individual blocks aren't enough on distributed teams

If I block my Tuesday morning as deep work but my colleague doesn't know that, they'll still send the urgent Slack message at 10:15am, still schedule the quick check-in I have to decide whether to decline.

Individual blocks create friction against meeting invites. They don't create shared awareness of when the team is in focused work vs. available for interruption.

## The shared focus time approach

Teams that protect distributed focus effectively usually have one of two things:

**Designated quiet hours.** A shared window (typically morning) where the norm is no meetings and slow Slack responses. Not a rule — a norm. "From 9–11am, we're all heads down. Slack messages will be seen by noon." Publicized, expected, respected.

**Shared focus calendar.** A team calendar (visible to all) where members post their focus blocks. When my focus block appears on the shared calendar, teammates can see it alongside their own schedules. The social visibility creates a different dynamic than an individual block that only the individual sees.

## The conversation it requires

Neither approach works without a team conversation. The norm needs to be proposed, discussed, and adopted — not assumed or quietly enacted by one person. That conversation usually takes 30 minutes and requires someone to bring it up.

The hardest part is often just starting the conversation. "I've noticed we're all getting a lot of meeting interruptions during what should be focus time. Can we discuss a shared approach?" — that's the sentence.

> Focus time norms are more durable on distributed teams when they're collective agreements, not individual practices. The team that agrees to protect mornings together is harder to interrupt than the individual who blocks their own morning.

---

**This week's takeaway:**
- Individual focus blocks help but don't create team-level awareness of when interruptions are costly
- Shared quiet hours or a team focus calendar creates visibility that changes collective behavior
- Starting the conversation is usually the bottleneck — it takes one person to propose it
