# How Async Scheduling Works When Your Team Spans 12 Time Zones

*Schedule Calendar Weekly · May 7, 2027*

---

Most scheduling tools are built for synchronous teams. You find a slot where everyone is available, send the invite, done. When a team spans 12 time zones, that model breaks. There is no slot where everyone is available without someone being awake at 3am.

Async scheduling is the alternative — and it requires a different approach to both calendar management and communication.

## The first principle: synchronous meetings become a premium

When meeting synchronously requires someone to be at a very inconvenient time, every meeting invitation is a cost imposed on the person in the difficult timezone. This makes meeting decisions more deliberate: you don't schedule a sync meeting unless you genuinely can't achieve the same outcome asynchronously.

This is actually a feature, not a bug. It filters out the meetings that were convenient but not necessary.

## Async scheduling in practice

**Pre-read documents replace most status updates.** Rather than meeting to share project updates, you share a short written update and invite async questions or comments. This works in any timezone because the reader engages on their schedule.

**Recorded video for communication that needs tone.** A 3-minute Loom video conveys nuance that text loses, and can be watched at any time. For announcements, context-setting, or emotional topics, this is better than both text and synchronous meetings.

**Shared decision documents for decisions.** A shared doc with a proposal, the options considered, and a deadline for input by a specific date. Whoever has the relevant perspective can contribute regardless of timezone.

**When a synchronous meeting is truly needed:** Rotate the burden. Don't have the San Francisco team always meet at normal hours while Tokyo joins at midnight. Alternate the inconvenient slot.

## The calendar's role in async scheduling

In a fully async workflow, the calendar primarily tracks: individual work blocks (to signal availability and protect focus time), the few synchronous meetings that justify the coordination cost, and personal/working-hours blocks that communicate availability to teammates.

> Async scheduling isn't less collaborative. It's differently collaborative — in a way that's often more inclusive for global teams.

---

**This week's takeaway:**
- Async scheduling makes every synchronous meeting a deliberate decision, not a default
- Pre-read documents, recorded video, and shared decision docs replace most status-update meetings
- When sync is required, rotate who bears the timezone burden
