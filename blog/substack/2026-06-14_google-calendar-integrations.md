# The Apps That Actually Make Google Calendar Better

*Schedule Calendar Weekly · October 30, 2026*

---

Google Calendar is the infrastructure layer. It stores your schedule, syncs across devices, and provides the foundation everything else connects to. But for most power users, a few integrations change how useful that foundation is.

I've tried a lot of calendar integrations over the years. Most weren't worth the setup cost. A few became permanent fixtures. Here's the honest version of which ones are worth it.

## Scheduling tools (worth it for anyone who books meetings externally)

Calendly and equivalents connect to Google Calendar and let you share a booking link instead of going back-and-forth on availability. The person picks from your available slots; the event appears automatically on both calendars.

The ROI calculation: if you schedule more than two external meetings per week, the time saved in email negotiation covers the subscription cost in the first month.

## Zoom / Google Meet / Teams integration (worth it for everyone)

Your video conferencing tool should be integrated so that meetings automatically include a join link. This is table stakes — if you're still adding meeting links manually, fix this today.

In Google Calendar, go to Settings → Add-ons to connect your conferencing provider. Once connected, new events have a join link by default.

## Task managers (worth it for specific workflows)

Todoist, Linear, Asana, and others can connect to Google Calendar to show scheduled tasks and deadlines as calendar events. Whether this is useful depends heavily on how you work. If your task list and calendar are two separate systems you toggle between, integration helps. If you time-block tasks manually into the calendar, duplication becomes a problem.

Test this for two weeks before committing.

## Chrome extensions for quick access (worth it for anyone using Calendar daily in a browser)

A browser extension that surfaces your calendar in a toolbar popup removes the need to switch tabs every time you need to check a time or find a join link. Small friction reduction, but it compounds.

> The right test for any integration: does it reduce the number of steps you take to do something you do every day? If yes, it earns its place. If it adds a new place to check without removing an existing one, it's overhead.

---

**This week's takeaway:**
- Scheduling tools (Calendly-type) earn their cost quickly for anyone with frequent external meetings
- Video conferencing integration is non-negotiable — add join links automatically, never manually
- Test task manager integration cautiously; duplication between systems creates new problems
