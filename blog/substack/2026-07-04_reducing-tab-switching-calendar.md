# I Was Opening Google Calendar 40 Times a Day. Here's How I Fixed It.

*Schedule Calendar Weekly · March 19, 2027*

---

The specific number comes from a tracking experiment I did with a tab counter extension: 42 times in one day I switched to the Google Calendar tab. 42.

At an average of 20–25 seconds per switch — finding the tab, loading the page if it needed a refresh, reading what I needed, switching back — that's roughly 15 minutes of switching, plus the attention cost of leaving my current context 42 times.

I was skeptical that a browser extension would matter. After a week with one installed, I tracked again: 8 tab switches to the full Google Calendar. The rest of the checks happened in the popup.

## Why this happens

Calendar checking is frequent because it answers questions that come up constantly in a knowledge worker's day:

- What time is my next meeting?
- Do I have time to take on this task?
- What's the join link for this call?
- Did that meeting get moved?
- How much time do I have before the end of the day?

None of these questions require the full Google Calendar interface. They require 5–15 seconds of access to a limited set of information: upcoming events, times, and links.

## The actual fix

A browser toolbar extension that shows the calendar in a popup satisfies all of these questions without a tab switch. The popup opens in under a second, shows the relevant events for today, and closes when you click away.

The full calendar tab remains available for actual planning, event creation, and anything requiring the weekly view. It just stops being the destination for 40+ daily quick checks.

## The compounding effect

The value isn't only the time. It's the reduced interruption to whatever you were doing when the question arose. Every tab switch is a micro-interruption — a moment where you left the work to check something and returned. Reducing those interruptions, even small ones, has a cumulative effect on the depth and quality of the work you're doing between meetings.

> Friction reduction tools are most valuable for high-frequency actions. The same 20-second savings means almost nothing once a week and means 10 minutes a day when the action happens 30+ times daily.

---

**This week's takeaway:**
- High-frequency calendar checking adds up; 30 checks at 25 seconds each is 12+ minutes daily
- A popup extension moves quick checks from tab switches to toolbar clicks — same information, fraction of the friction
- The secondary benefit is reduced context-switching for the work happening between calendar checks
