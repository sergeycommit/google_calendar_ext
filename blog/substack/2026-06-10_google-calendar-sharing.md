# Sharing Your Calendar Changes How Teams Coordinate. Here's How to Do It Well.

*Schedule Calendar Weekly · October 2, 2026*

---

At a previous job, the first thing I noticed was that nobody shared their calendars. Scheduling a meeting meant sending a poll, waiting for responses, finding a time that worked, sending the invite — a process that took two days for something that should take two minutes.

The second job I had, everyone shared calendars with availability visible to the whole team. Scheduling took thirty seconds: you opened someone's calendar, found a gap, sent the invite.

The difference in coordination friction was enormous. And the downside — some privacy trade-off — was genuinely minimal, because "see free/busy" sharing reveals almost nothing sensitive about what's in your calendar.

## The sharing levels in Google Calendar

Google Calendar offers three sharing levels worth understanding:

**See free/busy only:** Others see when you're available or busy, but not event titles or details. This is the floor — the minimum useful for coordinating across a team.

**See all event details:** Others see event titles, descriptions, attendees, and locations. More transparency, more coordination power, some privacy trade-off.

**Make changes to events:** Full edit access. Appropriate for an EA managing your calendar, not for general team sharing.

Most team setups should be "see free/busy" at minimum, with "see all event details" for close collaborators.

## How to share effectively

Go to Calendar Settings → find your calendar → Sharing with specific people. Add colleagues. Choose the level. Done.

For broader team visibility, Google Workspace administrators can configure default sharing within an organization. If you're not an admin, asking for this is worth the conversation with your IT team — the coordination benefit scales with adoption.

## What this doesn't mean

Shared calendars aren't an invitation to judge how other people spend their time. A colleague with a full calendar of deep work blocks isn't "less available" — they're being transparent about committed time. That transparency is the point.

> Calendar sharing is an act of trust that reduces coordination costs for everyone. Teams that share calendars stop losing time to scheduling logistics and spend it on the actual work.

---

**This week's takeaway:**
- Free/busy sharing is the minimum for team calendar coordination; it reveals almost no sensitive information
- Sharing reduces scheduling friction from days to seconds for people who can see each other's availability
- Event-detail sharing is appropriate for close collaborators; free/busy is usually enough for the broader team
