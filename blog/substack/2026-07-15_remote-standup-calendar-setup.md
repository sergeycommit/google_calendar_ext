# Building a Remote Standup Ritual That People Actually Show Up To

*Schedule Calendar Weekly · June 4, 2027*

---

Most remote standups die within three months. They start with good intentions — daily coordination, visibility, connection — and gradually become a box everyone checks without paying attention. Attendance drifts. People join late. The format ossifies. The meeting runs past its time. Eventually someone suggests cancelling it and everyone quietly agrees.

The standups that survive are designed differently.

## What kills remote standups

**Wrong format.** A text standup (everyone shares a bullet list in Slack) requires less coordination than a video call but often produces better output. A video standup justified by "we need the face time" rarely justifies its cost if the team meets regularly in other contexts.

**Wrong duration.** Fifteen minutes, strictly enforced, forces prioritization. Thirty-minute standups drift. Whatever the format, set a strict time limit and keep it.

**Wrong timing.** A standup at 9am isn't realistic for a distributed team unless you want half the team joining from bed. Find the overlap window — the time that's morning-ish for most people — and use it.

**No agenda.** "Updates" is not an agenda. A standup with a clear format — blockers, priorities, dependencies — is structurally different from one where people share whatever comes to mind.

## The calendar setup for a standup that runs well

**Set it for a time within the team's shared window.** If that window is narrow (1–2 hours), protect it jealously for the standup. Don't let other meetings colonize it.

**Make the event 15 minutes, not 30.** In Google Calendar, this requires either changing the default event duration or manually setting the end time. Fifteen minutes signals that you're serious about the format.

**Put the agenda in the event description.** Three questions: What did you work on since last standup? What are you working on today? Is anything blocking you? That's the entire format. It's in the invite. Nobody has to remember it.

**Make attendance non-negotiable or make it optional.** Ambiguous attendance norms produce inconsistent attendance. Decide which it is.

> A standup that runs 15 minutes and ends with everyone knowing the team's blockers is better than a 30-minute meeting that ends with everyone wondering why they were there.

---

**This week's takeaway:**
- Most remote standups fail because of wrong format, wrong duration, or wrong timing — fixable problems
- 15 minutes, strict format, agenda in the invite description: the structure that survives
- Decide clearly whether attendance is required or optional; ambiguity produces inconsistency
