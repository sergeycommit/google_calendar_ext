# When My Calendar System Had More Categories Than I Had Hours

*Schedule Calendar Weekly · January 29, 2027*

---

At some point last year, my calendar system had twelve color categories, four calendar layers, three sets of recurring blocks, and a notation system that required me to remember what a blue dot in the title meant.

It was a system that had been optimized to feel productive, not to be productive. I was spending more time maintaining the system than benefitting from it.

The simplification took 30 minutes and nothing important broke.

## How calendar systems become overcomplicated

It happens incrementally. You add a category for a new project. You add another for a different area of focus. You create a block type for one-off tasks. A color gets reassigned. Soon you have a system that requires documentation to use.

Every new layer was added for a reason. The problem is that the cumulative overhead of all the layers becomes the dominant cost.

## The simplification principle

A calendar system should answer two questions quickly: what do I have today, and when is my next available time for focused work?

If your system can't answer those questions faster than a plain list, it's over-engineered.

## What stayed after simplification

**Three colors.** Green for deep work, blue for meetings, orange for everything else. That's it. Not twelve.

**Two calendar layers.** Work calendar (shared), personal calendar (private). Previously I had four.

**One type of recurring block.** A morning focus block, recurring weekdays. Previously I had five different recurring block types with different rules.

**No notation in titles.** No symbols, no codes, no special markers. If a title needs a symbol to make sense, the title isn't specific enough.

## The result

I can read my calendar in three seconds. The visual pattern is immediately interpretable. I spend zero time maintaining the system.

> Simplicity is not the enemy of sophistication. A system that works with minimal overhead is more sophisticated than one that requires explanation. The sophistication is in the judgment that produced the simplicity.

---

**This week's takeaway:**
- Calendar system overhead grows incrementally and often invisibly — audit the system, not just the schedule
- Three color categories, two calendar layers, and one type of recurring block covers most needs
- A system that requires explanation is a system that needs simplification
