# The Week I Stopped Reacting and Started Blocking Time

*Schedule Calendar Weekly · April 3, 2026*

---

A few years ago I noticed something uncomfortable: every week felt like I was responding to other people's priorities. Meetings filled the calendar. Tasks kept sliding. By Friday, the list was longer than Monday. The problem wasn't that I was bad at my job — it was that I had no system for protecting time for the work that actually mattered.

Time blocking fixed that. Not perfectly, but substantially.

## What time blocking actually is (and isn't)

Time blocking is simple: you assign a specific calendar slot to a specific task. Not just meetings — actual work. A "Draft Q3 report" block at 9am on Tuesday gets the same calendar real estate as a meeting. It shows up visually. It crowds out other things.

The difference from a to-do list is critical. A task on a list sits there waiting for time to appear. A calendar block *is* the time. That shift — from "I'll get to it" to "this happens Tuesday 9–11am" — changes your relationship to the work.

## Three types of blocks I use every week

**Deep work blocks (90–120 min)** for anything requiring sustained concentration. I protect these like meetings. I mark them Busy. I don't open email during them.

**Shallow work batches (30–45 min)** for email, Slack, admin, forms. Batching these into one or two windows per day stops them from fragmenting everything else.

**Buffer blocks (15–20 min)** between dense items. A little white space before a difficult task or after a long call is worth more than you'd expect.

## The 60–70% rule

Here's what I got wrong for the first year: I over-blocked. I filled 90% of my hours with named tasks. Then one unexpected meeting would cascade and the whole week would feel like a failure.

The better approach: block 60–70% of your available time, not 100%. The gap absorbs the unexpected. An over-blocked week collapses the moment anything changes.

> The remaining 30–40% isn't wasted slack. It's the structural resilience that makes the whole system survive contact with reality.

## How to set it up in Google Calendar

Create an event, give it a specific task name ("Finish API docs section 3", not "Focus time"), set a distinct color from your meetings, and mark it Busy. For recurring work, use the repeat function — a daily deep work block is more durable than one you recreate each week.

---

**This week's takeaway:**
- Name every block with a specific task, not just a category
- Block 60–70% of available hours — leave structural room for the unexpected
- Mark focus blocks as Busy to create friction against meeting conflicts
