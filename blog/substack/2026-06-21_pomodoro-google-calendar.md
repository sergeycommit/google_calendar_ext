# Combining Pomodoro With Calendar Blocks Is Better Than Either Alone

*Schedule Calendar Weekly · December 18, 2026*

---

Pomodoro and time blocking are both productivity systems, and people who use one often haven't tried combining them with the other. I spent a year using each in isolation before using them together. The combination is meaningfully better.

## What each one does

**Time blocking** solves the scheduling problem: *when* will I work on what? It claims time on the calendar, creates visibility, and prevents meetings from filling every available hour.

**The Pomodoro Technique** solves the execution problem: *how* will I use the time once I'm in it? A 25-minute focused sprint, a 5-minute break, repeat. The constraint creates urgency; the break creates recovery.

The gap: time blocking tells you when to work but doesn't help you stay focused during the block. Pomodoro tells you how to stay focused but doesn't protect the time for you to use it.

## How the combination works

When a 90-minute work block starts, I run three Pomodoros: 25 min work / 5 min break / 25 min work / 5 min break / 25 min work / 5 min break. The block gives me the protected time; the Pomodoro structure gives me the internal cadence.

The break between Pomodoros is the detail most people miss. It's not optional. It's what makes the next sprint possible at the same quality as the first. Standing, walking, getting water — not checking email or Slack, which restarts the attention cost you just paid to focus.

## The calendar configuration

I don't track Pomodoros on the calendar. The block is the unit of planning; the Pomodoros are the unit of execution within the block. Trying to schedule individual 25-minute slots in the calendar adds overhead without adding value.

One adjustment that helps: if my block is 90 minutes, I don't end the calendar event at 90 minutes precisely. I end it at 85 minutes and leave 5 minutes for transition. That final 5 minutes is for writing down where I left off — so the next session doesn't start cold.

> The system works because it answers two different questions: the calendar answers "when do I do focused work?" and the Pomodoro answers "how do I actually focus for 90 minutes?" Neither question answered by the other.

---

**This week's takeaway:**
- Time blocking and Pomodoro solve different halves of the productivity problem: scheduling vs. execution
- Pomodoro runs inside the calendar block, not as an alternative to it
- The break between Pomodoros is structural, not optional — it enables the next sprint's quality
