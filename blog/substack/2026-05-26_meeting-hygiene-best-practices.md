# The One Meeting Rule I Wish I'd Started With

*Schedule Calendar Weekly · June 19, 2026*

---

If I could give one piece of advice to someone who manages a lot of meetings, it would be this: never send a meeting invite without an agenda.

Not a paragraph-long agenda. Not a detailed document. Just one or two sentences that answer: what decision or outcome are we trying to reach, and who needs to be there for it?

That single rule, applied consistently, changes how meetings run. People arrive with the right context. The first ten minutes stop being spent figuring out why everyone is in the room. Someone actually owns the outcome.

## What meeting hygiene actually means

Meeting hygiene is the set of habits that determine whether a meeting earns its place on the calendar. Poor hygiene shows up as: vague reasons for gathering, attendees who don't know why they were invited, decisions that don't get made, and follow-ups that don't happen.

The goal isn't formality. It's purposefulness. When you block an hour on someone else's calendar, that's an hour of their life. Meeting hygiene is how you justify the ask.

## The eight habits that actually change things

1. Send the agenda 24 hours before — even one bullet point is enough to orient people
2. Name a meeting owner who is responsible for outcomes, not just logistics
3. State the meeting type upfront: decision, brainstorm, update, or review
4. Invite only people who have a role — every extra attendee has a cost
5. Start on time, every time
6. Keep a live notes document visible during the call
7. End with explicit recap: decisions made, next steps, owners, deadlines
8. Follow up within two hours with a written summary

## The agenda problem

The biggest leverage point is the agenda — not because it's the most impactful in isolation, but because writing it forces a clarity check before the meeting happens.

If you cannot write a one-sentence agenda for a meeting, that's a signal the meeting isn't ready to happen. You don't know what outcome you're seeking. That usually means the meeting will drift, run long, and produce nothing actionable.

> Before sending any invite, ask: can I finish this sentence? "This meeting succeeds if we ___________." If you can't, reschedule or replace the meeting with an async message.

---

**This week's takeaway:**
- An agenda — even one bullet — changes how participants prepare and how the meeting runs
- Name one owner; diffuse ownership produces no accountability
- End every meeting with explicit decisions and next steps written down
