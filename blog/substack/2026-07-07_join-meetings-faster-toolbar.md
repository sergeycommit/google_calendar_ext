# The Join Button That Saved Me From Being 3 Minutes Late to Every Call

*Schedule Calendar Weekly · April 9, 2027*

---

For a year and a half, I had the same routine for joining meetings: notification fires, I open the Calendar tab, I find the event, I expand it, I hunt for the Zoom/Meet link buried in the event description, I click it, the browser asks if I want to open the app, I click yes, the app opens.

From notification to meeting: 90 seconds on a good day. Longer when I couldn't find the link or it was a poorly formatted invite.

I joined most calls 1–3 minutes late. Not late enough to apologize about, but late enough to miss the opening, late enough to have everyone notice the notification sound of someone joining mid-sentence.

## The actual problem

The meeting join link is in the calendar. The calendar is in another tab. Getting to the link requires finding the tab, finding the event, and extracting the link from the event description where it's often mixed with notes, dial-in numbers, and conference IDs.

This is an information retrieval problem in a system that shouldn't require retrieval at that moment.

## The fix

A calendar browser extension that surfaces upcoming events in a popup, with one-click join buttons, eliminates the entire sequence. The popup shows "Meeting starts in 3 min — Join" as a direct button. One click.

The join link doesn't need to be found. It's presented in the context where you need it.

## The second-order effect

When joining is effortless, I join on time. When I join on time, I'm present for the opening context. When I'm present for the opening, I contribute more effectively. The 90-second reduction in joining friction has a downstream effect on meeting quality.

This probably sounds like I'm overstating a small thing. But "join on time" is one of those meetings habits that has compounding effects on professional reputation and meeting culture. Being the person who joins late to every call is a signal, however small, that the caller's time matters more than everyone else's.

> Tools that remove friction from unavoidable daily actions have asymmetrically large effects on professional habits.

---

**This week's takeaway:**
- Hunting for meeting join links from the calendar tab is a daily friction point that compounds
- A popup extension with one-click join reduces the workflow from 6 steps to 1
- Consistently joining on time has second-order effects on meeting culture and professional impression
