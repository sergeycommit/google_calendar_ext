# What the Data Says About Meetings, Focus Time, and Productivity

*Schedule Calendar Weekly · February 11, 2028*

---

Individual productivity advice is often anecdotal. "Here's what worked for me" is useful but not definitive. Over the past few years, several large-scale studies of calendar and meeting patterns have produced data worth knowing — because the patterns they identify appear to apply broadly.

## The headline findings

**Meeting load has increased substantially.** Microsoft's Work Trend Index and Harvard Business Review research consistently show that knowledge worker meeting time roughly doubled between 2020 and 2025, and has not returned to pre-pandemic levels. The average knowledge worker spends 35–40% of their work time in meetings.

**Focus time is rare.** The same research finds that uninterrupted blocks of 2+ hours are increasingly uncommon. Most workers have fewer than two such blocks per week.

**Small group meetings are more productive.** Meeting effectiveness data consistently shows that meetings with 5 or fewer people make decisions and produce outcomes more reliably than larger groups. Meetings with 10+ participants rarely produce consensus.

**Recovery time between meetings matters.** Studies on back-to-back meetings show measurable stress markers that accumulate across consecutive calls and take 20–25 minutes to return to baseline after a meeting ends. Ten-minute buffers prevent this accumulation.

**No-meeting days improve output.** Research from Asana and Microsoft both found that even one no-meeting day per week produced measurable improvements in reported productivity, focus quality, and employee satisfaction.

## What this means practically

The data validates what individual experience suggests: less time in meetings + more protected focus time = better output and lower stress. The data also suggests that these conditions are increasingly rare — which means deliberately creating them is increasingly valuable.

> Individual calendar optimization isn't just personal preference. It's swimming against a measurable organizational current.
