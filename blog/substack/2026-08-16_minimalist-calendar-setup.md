# The Minimalist Calendar: What Happens When You Strip Away Everything Non-Essential

*Schedule Calendar Weekly · January 14, 2028*

---

At the start of last year I did an experiment: I removed everything from my calendar that wasn't strictly necessary and started from zero. No recurring blocks (except meetings I was required to attend), no elaborate color system, no planning framework.

Just the events that were actually happening.

Then I added things back, one at a time, only when their absence created a specific problem.

## What the minimalist base revealed

The calendar with only essential events showed me something uncomfortable: most of the complexity I'd accumulated wasn't helping. It was infrastructure I'd built to manage complexity I'd also accumulated. The two were feeding each other.

A calendar with 12 event categories managed 12 types of events. When I dropped to 3 categories, the events didn't become harder to manage — the 12-category overhead just disappeared.

## What I added back (and why)

**Color coding (2 colors, not 12).** Green for focused work. Blue for meetings. Everything else is default gray. This returned the visual scanning value without the taxonomy overhead.

**Morning deep work block (recurring).** The absence was immediately noticeable — without it, meetings claimed the morning by default within days. This earned its place back.

**Weekly planning session (recurring).** Same reason. Without the explicit review event, weeks started without a plan.

**Task-specific names on blocks.** Not just "focus" but "Draft proposal section 2." This returned without requiring a formal system.

Everything else — the buffer blocks, the shallow work batches, the elaborate color taxonomy, the multiple recurring review events — I left out. The calendar is measurably simpler. Nothing important has broken.

## The minimalist principle

The minimum viable calendar is the one that stops the specific problems you've actually experienced. If back-to-back meetings aren't exhausting you, buffer blocks aren't necessary. If mornings aren't getting claimed, the morning block might not be essential. Design for your actual problems.

> The best calendar setup for you is the simplest one that solves your actual problems. Not the theoretically complete one.

---

**This week's takeaway:**
- Most calendar complexity is infrastructure for managing other complexity — when you reduce both, little breaks
- Start minimal; add one element when its absence creates a specific, recurring problem
- The minimum viable calendar: commitments, one type of focus block, a weekly planning event
