# Set It Once, Benefit Forever: Using Recurring Events as Calendar Infrastructure

*Schedule Calendar Weekly · September 18, 2026*

---

The most reliable parts of my calendar are the ones I created once and haven't touched since.

My morning deep work block, my end-of-day review, my weekly planning session, my team standup, my Friday retrospective — all recurring events, all running automatically, all contributing to the rhythm of the week without requiring a decision each time they appear.

That's the hidden value of recurring events. They're not just convenient. They're infrastructure.

## What calendar infrastructure means

Infrastructure is the stuff that runs quietly in the background, enabling everything built on top of it. A good recurring event is infrastructure: it's the regular slot that anchors the week, ensures the important things happen without relying on willpower, and creates predictability that other people (and your future self) can plan around.

The alternative — deciding each week whether to have a deep work block, when to plan, whether to do a retrospective — adds cognitive load and introduces variability. Good infrastructure removes those decisions.

## What to make recurring

Not everything should be recurring. Specific project tasks, one-time meetings, and deadlines don't belong in recurring series. But these categories do:

**Weekly anchors:** A planning session (mine is 30 minutes Friday afternoon), an end-of-week review, a personal check-in on goals.

**Daily habits:** Morning deep work, afternoon shallow work batch, end-of-day shutdown routine.

**Team rhythms:** Standups, team syncs, cross-functional check-ins.

**Health and maintenance:** Exercise, lunch, commute windows — whatever you need to protect in the calendar to ensure it actually happens.

## Managing recurring events well

The main risk with recurring events is staleness. Set up a recurring meeting six months ago for a project that wrapped three months ago? It's still running. This is where the recurring meeting audit from a few weeks ago comes in.

I do a quick scan of all recurring events every quarter: does each still serve its purpose? Is the frequency still right? Is the invite list still correct?

> The other thing to get right: the RSVP status on your own recurring events. If a block is for your personal work, set it to Busy. If it's a hold you created for a colleague who may want to cancel, set it to Free. The status communicates availability to anyone who checks your calendar.

---

**This week's takeaway:**
- Recurring events are calendar infrastructure: set up once, deliver reliably
- Weekly anchors, daily habits, and team rhythms are the best candidates for recurring events
- Review all recurring events quarterly to prune what's outlived its purpose
