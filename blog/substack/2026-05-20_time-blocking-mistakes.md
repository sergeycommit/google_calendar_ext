# 5 Time Blocking Mistakes I Made (And How to Avoid Them)

*Schedule Calendar Weekly · May 8, 2026*

---

Time blocking sounded simple when I first heard about it. Add tasks to the calendar, protect the time, do the work. It took about three weeks to discover how many ways there are to get it wrong.

Here are the five mistakes I made, roughly in the order I made them.

## Mistake 1: Over-blocking to 100%

I treated time blocking as a scheduling optimization problem. More blocks = more productivity. By the end of the first week, my calendar looked like a packed subway car.

Then a meeting ran long, one task took twice as long as I planned, and the entire system fell apart by 2pm. Everything pushed. Nothing adjusted cleanly. I ended the day feeling more behind than before I started.

The fix: block 60–70% of available time. The remaining 30–40% isn't wasted — it's structural buffer that lets the plan survive contact with reality.

## Mistake 2: Vague block names

"Focus time" is not a task. "Deep work" is not a plan. When I sat down to a vaguely-named block, I'd spend the first 10 minutes figuring out what I was supposed to be doing — often defaulting to email because that was easier than deciding.

The fix: every block gets a specific task name. "Draft intro section of Q3 report." Not "work on report."

## Mistake 3: Not marking blocks as Busy

A Free block on my calendar was treated by the scheduling universe as an open invitation. New meeting requests slid in without any friction. Within days, most of my blocks had been displaced.

The fix: Busy status. It doesn't make the block permanent, but it forces an active decision to move it rather than a passive default.

## Mistake 4: Scheduling hard work at low-energy times

I was blocking deep work at 4pm because that's when meetings weren't scheduled. But 4pm is when my thinking is slowest. The block existed; nothing good came out of it.

The fix: match task difficulty to energy level. Peak thinking work in peak hours — typically morning. Admin and shallow work in low-energy windows.

## Mistake 5: Never reviewing which blocks actually ran

I kept creating blocks but never checked whether they were working. Some topics kept getting blocked and repeatedly moved — a signal that something structural was wrong, not just a scheduling issue.

The fix: a short weekly review. Which blocks ran? Which kept moving? Which tasks need more time allocated? The data is all there in the calendar.

---

**This week's takeaway:**
- Over-blocking is the most common failure mode — leave 30–40% unblocked
- Specific task names create accountability that category names don't
- Check energy levels when placing demanding blocks; 4pm deep work rarely works
