# Stop Scheduling by Clock. Start Scheduling by Energy.

*Schedule Calendar Weekly · May 22, 2026*

---

For a long time, I scheduled tasks based on when I had available time. If there was a free slot at 3pm, that's when I'd put the thing I needed to write, design, or analyze. The time was there; the task needed doing. Seemed logical.

The problem: 3pm me is a different person than 9am me. Not philosophically — neurologically. Cognitive performance varies across the day, and some tasks need the version of me that hasn't yet made a hundred small decisions.

## The basic biology behind energy-based scheduling

Most people have a cognitive peak — a 2–4 hour window when focus is sharpest and decision quality is highest. For roughly 80% of people this is in the late morning. For true evening types it shifts later. But the window exists and it's measurable.

After that peak, there's a mid-afternoon trough (the post-lunch dip is real), and a smaller secondary peak in the late afternoon for many people.

Scheduling high-demand work during the trough and low-demand work during the peak is — in productivity terms — wasteful. But that's exactly what happens when scheduling is driven by availability rather than energy.

## Mapping tasks to energy levels

I categorize everything I do into three rough levels:

**High cognitive demand:** Writing, complex problem-solving, code architecture, strategy work, creative work. These need the peak.

**Medium cognitive demand:** Meetings, reviews, collaborative work, editing. These work in the shoulder periods — late morning before or after the peak, or mid-afternoon when it's not too depleted.

**Low cognitive demand:** Email, Slack, admin, data entry, scheduling, expense reports. These go in the trough. They don't need my best thinking, so there's no cost to putting them in the worst window.

## What this looks like on a calendar

I block my peak window (8:30–10:30am) for high-demand work every day. No meetings in that window without a genuinely urgent reason. After 10:30, I open up to meetings and collaborative work. After 4pm, I batch my low-demand tasks.

The calendar looks the same as any other time-blocked calendar. The difference is that the task type matches the energy level of the slot, not just the availability of the time.

> One honest caveat: you have to actually know when your peak is. Pay attention for a week. When do you do your best thinking? When does your mind wander? The answer is personal, and it's worth knowing.

---

**This week's takeaway:**
- Availability and energy are different resources; scheduling should use both
- High-demand tasks belong in your cognitive peak, not your first free slot
- Low-demand work is the ideal use of your trough — it's not wasted time, it's strategic placement

*When is your best thinking window? If you're not sure, track it for three days. You'll see the pattern.*
