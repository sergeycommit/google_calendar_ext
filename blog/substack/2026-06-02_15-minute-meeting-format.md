# I Switched All My Standup Meetings to 15 Minutes. Here's What Happened.

*Schedule Calendar Weekly · August 7, 2026*

---

Our team standup had been 30 minutes for two years. Nobody had ever questioned it. Thirty minutes was what standups were. We'd scheduled them that way when the team was smaller and used all the time, and somewhere along the way it became permanent.

I asked if we could try 15 minutes for a month. The team was skeptical. What actually happened: the meetings got tighter, decisions happened faster, and nobody reported missing the other 15 minutes.

## Why constraint improves meeting quality

When a meeting has 30 minutes, it takes 30 minutes. When it has 15, things that don't need to be said out loud don't get said. Status updates that could be async stay async. The meeting becomes the actual coordination layer rather than a combination of coordination, status theater, and social catch-up.

This is Parkinson's Law applied to meetings: work expands to fill the time available. The inverse is also true. Compress the time, and the meeting compresses around what actually matters.

## The 15-minute format that works

**Minutes 0–2:** What did I do since we last met? (Each person, one sentence)
**Minutes 2–10:** What's blocking someone right now? (Only blockers — anything that needs discussion)
**Minutes 10–14:** Decisions and next steps from the blockers
**Minute 15:** Done

Anything that isn't a blocker doesn't belong in the standup. It goes in a doc, a Slack message, or a separate conversation. The 15-minute format survives only if the team respects this boundary.

## What to do with things that spill over

The standup will regularly surface something that needs more discussion than 15 minutes allows. That's fine — and it's actually useful information. Those conversations get a separate slot with the right people, rather than being resolved in a full-team call where most attendees have no stake in the outcome.

> A 15-minute standup that consistently surfaces "we need to talk further about X" is doing its job. It's a triage layer, not a decision layer.

## When 15 minutes doesn't work

Very complex projects with many interdependencies sometimes genuinely need more time. That's real. But most teams discover that 15 minutes covers what 30 was doing, and the "we need more time" conversations tend to be about a specific issue, not the format itself.

---

**This week's takeaway:**
- Shorter meeting durations force prioritization of what actually belongs in a meeting
- The 15-minute standup format: yesterday/today (brief), blockers (main event), next steps (close)
- Topics that need more discussion get a separate conversation with the right people, not extra standup time

*Try halving the duration of one recurring meeting. You'll know within two weeks whether it works.*
