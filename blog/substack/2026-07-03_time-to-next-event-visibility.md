# Seeing "Time Until Next Event" Changed How I Manage the Gap Between Meetings

*Schedule Calendar Weekly · March 12, 2027*

---

Before I set up a persistent "time to next event" display in my browser toolbar, I had a particular bad habit: starting tasks that were too big for the available window.

I'd have 40 minutes before a meeting, think "I'll start this project spec," and then spend 20 minutes getting oriented before realizing I had 20 minutes left and nothing complete. The interruption cost was high, and the partial work was often harder to restart than if I hadn't started at all.

## What persistent time-to-next-event visibility does

A small "Next: in 38 min" displayed in the browser toolbar creates a constant, ambient awareness of how much time is available between now and the next commitment. No clicking required — it's just there, like a clock.

With this visible, I make different decisions:

- With 90 minutes: I start deep work and commit to it.
- With 45 minutes: I pick a bounded task I can finish, or I batch the shallow work.
- With 20 minutes: I handle email, review notes, or do explicit prep for the upcoming meeting.
- With 8 minutes: I don't start anything new. I prepare.

## Why this is different from checking the calendar

Checking the calendar is a discrete action. Ambient display is continuous awareness. The distinction matters because the decision about how to use the next 30 minutes happens constantly — every time you finish one thing and consider starting another. That decision is made better when the relevant information is always visible, not only when you remember to look it up.

## The information asymmetry problem

Without time visibility, we systematically underestimate how much time is left before commitments. We start things that don't fit. We don't prepare for things that need preparation. The ambient display solves an information problem, not a motivation one.

> The change it produces is subtle and consistent: better matching of task size to available window. Not transformative in any individual moment — just consistently better across every transition in the day.

**This week's takeaway:**
- Ambient time-to-next-event visibility enables better task-to-window matching throughout the day
- The benefit is in the transitions: every time you finish one thing, you have the context to choose the next appropriately
- This is an information problem, not a motivation problem — the right data changes behavior automatically
