# The 45-Minute Batch That Saved My Morning Attention

*Schedule Calendar Weekly · June 5, 2026*

---

For a long time, I handled email and Slack reactively — checking whenever I saw a notification, or whenever a task got hard and I needed a mental escape hatch. The inbox was always open in a background tab. I'd glance at it between tasks, and before I knew it I was three email threads deep and 40 minutes had passed.

The thing I didn't realize: this wasn't just inefficient, it was fragmenting the attention I needed for everything else. Every time I glanced at email, I introduced the possibility of a new concern, a new request, a new problem to carry in the back of my mind while I tried to do something else.

## What batching shallow work actually does

Shallow work batching is the practice of grouping all low-cognitive-demand tasks — email, Slack, admin, forms, expense reports — into specific windows, and handling them all at once rather than reactively throughout the day.

Two batches per day is usually enough for most roles: one in mid-morning (after the peak deep work window), one in the late afternoon. Outside those windows, notifications are off or ignored.

The mechanism: instead of interrupting your thinking repeatedly with small tasks, you bank all the small tasks and deal with them in one go. The batch is complete, the inbox is clear (or managed), and then it closes again.

## What changes

The most surprising effect wasn't time saved — it was the quality of attention during the rest of the day. When I know I'm not supposed to check email until 10:30, I don't check email until 10:30. The option isn't on the table. That cognitive closure — not having open loops wondering if something came in — is worth more than the literal minutes.

> It also makes the batches themselves more efficient. Email handled in a focused 40-minute window gets processed faster than the same emails handled across 15 micro-interruptions, because you build and maintain context rather than constantly reloading it.

## Setting it up on the calendar

I have two recurring blocks: "Shallow work batch" at 10:30–11:15 and 4:15–5:00. Both marked with a specific color (yellow), both marked Free (these aren't focus blocks — it's fine if meetings occasionally push them). The names are a signal to myself, not a structural protection.

The rule I follow: if something in the batch will take more than 15 minutes, it gets its own block later. Don't let a long email thread turn a shallow batch into a deep work session.

---

**This week's takeaway:**
- Reactive email/Slack processing fragments attention across the whole day
- Two 45-minute batches is enough to handle most communication without being unreachable
- The cognitive benefit is larger than the time saved: fewer open loops means cleaner focus

*Try it for just three days. Pick two windows, close the inbox outside them, and notice what the first hour of the morning feels like.*
