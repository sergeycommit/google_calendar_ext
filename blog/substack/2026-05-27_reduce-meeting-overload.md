# I Audited 3 Months of My Calendar. Here's What I Found.

*Schedule Calendar Weekly · June 26, 2026*

---

At the start of this year, I did something I'd been avoiding: I opened the past three months of my calendar and actually counted my meetings.

The result was unpleasant. On average, I was in 22 hours of meetings per week. For context, a standard 40-hour week, minus lunch, minus commute transitions, leaves maybe 32 hours of "working time." I was spending more than two-thirds of it in meetings.

The worse realization: most of those meetings were recurring. I hadn't actively scheduled them recently. They'd been added months ago and were just... still happening. Automatically. Every week.

## The meeting creep problem

Meetings accumulate gradually. A weekly sync gets added. A new project starts a standup. A check-in becomes recurring "to keep the momentum." Nobody explicitly decided that 22 hours of meetings per week was the right number. It just became the number.

And because each meeting was added for a legitimate reason, no one had permission to question whether the collection still made sense.

## What the audit revealed

When I went through each recurring meeting with one question — "what would happen if this didn't exist?" — the answers sorted into three buckets:

**Keep:** Meetings where something concrete would break without them. About 40% of my recurring meetings fell here.

**Modify:** Meetings that served a purpose but had become too long, too frequent, or too broadly attended. About 35%.

**Cancel:** Meetings that had outlived their original purpose but kept running on inertia. About 25%.

I declined, cancelled, or restructured the last two categories. My weekly meeting load dropped from 22 hours to about 13. A week later, no major project suffered. A few people said they felt the same way.

## How to run your own audit

Set aside 45 minutes. Open your calendar. Look at every recurring meeting and ask: what would break if this stopped? Be honest. If the answer is "nothing immediate," that's the candidate.

You don't need to cancel everything. Modifying is often enough — a weekly that becomes bi-weekly, an hour that becomes 30 minutes, a 12-person meeting that really needs 5.

> The question isn't "is this meeting valuable?" Almost any meeting can claim value. The question is: "is this meeting the best use of this time, for everyone in the room?"

---

**This week's takeaway:**
- Recurring meetings accumulate by default; audit them intentionally at least quarterly
- Ask "what breaks if this stops?" — the honest answer reveals which meetings are load-bearing
- Modification is often enough: shorter duration, lower frequency, smaller invite list
