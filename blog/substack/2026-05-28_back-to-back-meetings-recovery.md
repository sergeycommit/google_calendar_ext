# Why I Build 10-Minute Gaps Between Every Meeting

*Schedule Calendar Weekly · July 3, 2026*

---

I used to schedule meetings back-to-back as a form of efficiency. Get them done, knock them out, free up the afternoon. What I didn't account for was what it cost to do that.

After three or four back-to-back meetings — each requiring different context, different relationships, different problems — I'd reach 2pm feeling like I'd run a cognitive sprint with no water break. The afternoon productivity I'd been trying to protect was gone before it started.

## What happens in the gap between meetings

That 10-minute window between calls isn't nothing. It's where several important things happen:

**Context switching.** Your brain needs a moment to let go of the previous meeting's problems and prepare for the next one's. Without that moment, you arrive at meeting two still mentally in meeting one.

**Note processing.** Immediately after a meeting is when your memory of decisions and commitments is sharpest. A five-minute buffer to write down what you just agreed to takes the load off your working memory before the next meeting overwrites it.

**Physical reset.** Stand up. Get water. Walk to a different room. These aren't luxuries — they change the physiological state you bring into the next conversation.

## How to build gaps structurally

The simplest method: when you create or accept a meeting invite, set your default duration in Google Calendar to end 10 minutes before the hour. A 60-minute meeting becomes 50 minutes. A 30-minute slot becomes 25.

This is often called the "speedy meetings" setting. It creates structural gaps without requiring you to negotiate individual meetings.

The other approach: block 10-minute "transition" events between dense meeting stretches. These look silly on a calendar but they're genuinely protective — they're marked Busy, so nothing can fill them, and they serve as both a physical break and a note-taking window.

> The most common objection is "but I have a lot of meetings and I need the time." The actual loss is 10 minutes per hour. The actual gain is arriving at each meeting mentally present rather than half-present and running on fumes.

## For really dense days

On days with more than four hours of meetings, I build in at least one 30-minute hard stop between the morning block and the afternoon block. Even on the densest days, a real break at midday changes the quality of the second half.

**This week's takeaway:**
- Back-to-back meetings create cumulative cognitive debt that appears in your afternoon productivity
- 10-minute gaps enable context switching, note taking, and physical reset
- Speedy meetings setting in Google Calendar automates this without requiring per-meeting negotiation
