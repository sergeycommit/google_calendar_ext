# Your Best Hours Are Probably Wasted on the Wrong Things

*Schedule Calendar Weekly · April 10, 2026*

---

There's a window in most people's day when thinking is sharpest. The ideas come faster, the writing flows, the hard problem suddenly has a solution. Most people know what that window is. Most people don't schedule anything important into it.

Instead, the high-energy hours fill with emails. Slack pings. A quick check-in that runs long. Status meetings. The genuinely hard work gets pushed to the afternoon, when the tank is half-empty.

I did this for years. The realization was uncomfortable.

## Why deep work blocks disappear from calendars

They get scheduled last — into the gaps between other things. They're marked Free, so new invites slip in without any resistance. They carry vague names like "Focus time" that provide no accountability when you sit down and feel the pull of the inbox instead.

By Wednesday, the block you planned is gone. Moved for a "quick call." Filled with email catch-up. Or just quietly not started because the day got away from you.

## Scheduling the block before anything else

The practical fix is uncomfortable if you're used to being responsive: schedule your deep work blocks *before* you open your inbox. Before you check Slack. Before you accept new invites.

The best hours on your calendar should go to the work that requires your best thinking — not to whatever happened to get scheduled first.

> A useful constraint: plan next week's deep work blocks on Friday or Sunday. The further in advance a block exists, the harder it is to displace. Urgency looks different at 9pm Sunday than it does at 9am Wednesday.

## Making the block harder to cancel

**Mark it Busy.** Not Free. When someone tries to book over a Busy block, they see a conflict. You can still move it — but you have to make an active decision rather than a passive one.

**Name it specifically.** "Write competitive analysis section 2" is harder to skip than "Deep work." The name is a mild commitment device. When you sit down, you know exactly what you're doing.

**Give it 90 minutes minimum.** The first 20 minutes of any deep session often goes to context recovery — finding where you were, remembering what the problem is. Blocks shorter than 60 minutes rarely reach the productive state worth protecting.

---

**This week's takeaway:**
- Schedule deep work into your peak cognitive hours, before meetings claim them
- Mark deep work blocks Busy — add friction to cancellation
- Name blocks specifically; vague names create no accountability
