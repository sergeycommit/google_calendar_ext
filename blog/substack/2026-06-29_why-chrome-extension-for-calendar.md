# Why I Added a Browser Extension to My Calendar (And What It Changed)

*Schedule Calendar Weekly · February 12, 2027*

---

Before I explain why I started using a calendar browser extension, let me describe the workflow I was using instead.

Working in the browser on a project. Need to check my next meeting. Switch to the Google Calendar tab. Read the calendar. Remember the time. Switch back to my work. Three minutes later, need to check again because I forgot the time. Repeat.

I was doing this roughly 15–20 times a day. Each instance took 15–30 seconds of actual time plus the context-switch cost of leaving the work I was doing. Add it up and it was 10–15 minutes of daily overhead for a task that should take 1 second.

## What a calendar browser extension actually does

A browser extension for Google Calendar puts the calendar in a toolbar popup. Click the icon in your browser toolbar; your calendar appears. See your events, get the join links, check times. Click away; the popup closes. You never left your current tab.

The entire interaction is 3–5 seconds instead of 30–60.

## What changed

The obvious change: time saved. Less obvious: the calendar became easier to *check* casually, which meant I actually checked it before making commitments rather than after.

When checking the calendar is a 3-second action, I check it before saying "sure, send the invite" to a scheduling request. When it requires a tab switch and a page load, I sometimes just say yes and hope there's no conflict. The lower friction produces better decisions.

## The things I actually use it for

- Checking the time of my next meeting (most common, multiple times a day)
- Getting the join link without hunting through the invite
- Quick look at the rest of the day before starting something new
- Checking if a proposed time is actually free

These are all simple actions. But simple actions done 15 times a day add up — and the right tool makes them genuinely frictionless.

> The principle: if you're doing something more than ten times a day, the quality of the tool matters more than you'd expect. Even 20 seconds saved per interaction is significant at that frequency.

---

**This week's takeaway:**
- Browser extensions for Google Calendar reduce tab-switching from a 30-second process to a 3-second one
- Lower friction for checking the calendar produces better real-time scheduling decisions
- The ROI compounds with frequency — any action done 15+ times daily benefits disproportionately from tooling improvements

*[Schedule Calendar](https://chromewebstore.google.com/detail/google-calendar-extension/dfbpjijneaihingmldgpgcodglkoamoe) is the extension I use — free, takes 30 seconds to install.*
