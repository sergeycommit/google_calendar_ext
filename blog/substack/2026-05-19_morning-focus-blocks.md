# How I Protect the First 90 Minutes of Every Day

*Schedule Calendar Weekly · May 1, 2026*

---

The most valuable hour of my workday used to start at 9am and end at 9:11am — when Slack opened, email loaded, and the first notification arrived.

From there, the rest of the morning was recovery mode. I'd spend it switching between other people's priorities, and by 11 I'd wonder where the time went. Nothing hard had started. Nothing hard would get done that day.

The change that worked: I stopped treating the morning as a time to settle in and start treating it as the only window where I could count on uninterrupted attention.

## Why mornings specifically

The science on this is fairly consistent: decision fatigue is real, cognitive peaks exist, and for most people they cluster in the first few hours after waking. That window is finite. Once it's used for email and notifications, it doesn't regenerate.

The practical consequence: whatever you schedule in your morning is going to get done with your best thinking. Whatever you schedule in the afternoon gets done with what's left.

## What the first 90 minutes actually looks like now

I block 8:30–10:00 every morning. The block has a specific task name — not "deep work" but whatever the most important piece of work is that week. I don't open my inbox before the block ends. I don't check Slack. I treat the first notification as a choice I'm making, not a reflex I'm following.

The block is marked Busy. It shows as unavailable to anyone who can schedule me. This isn't antisocial — it's the same thing a meeting would do to that slot.

> There's a version of this that sounds extreme: "no email before 11am." But it's less extreme than it sounds. Most messages don't need a same-morning response. Most things that feel urgent at 8am look different by 10.

## The setup in Google Calendar

A daily recurring event, specific name, color distinct from my meetings (I use green), set to Busy. Takes two minutes to set up, works automatically every day.

If something genuinely urgent needs to move it, I move it — and immediately reschedule it to a later window the same day. The block doesn't disappear; it relocates.

---

**This week's takeaway:**
- Mornings are your highest-quality attention window — protect them before meetings claim them
- Name the block specifically: what exactly will happen in that time?
- Treat it as Busy; don't let it be the default gap where meeting invites land
