# A Simpler Calendar Is Almost Always a Better Calendar

*Schedule Calendar Weekly · November 19, 2027*

---

There's an assumption embedded in most productivity advice that more sophistication produces better outcomes. More categories, more granularity, more structure — better visibility, better planning, better results.

My experience is the opposite. The calendar setups that work best are the simple ones. They work because they're fast to read, easy to maintain, and don't require the user to remember a system.

## What simplicity buys

**Speed of reading.** A simple calendar is readable in one glance. Three colors: green (focus), blue (meetings), orange (other). You can tell what any day looks like in two seconds.

**Maintainability.** A simple system is easy to keep current. When creating a new event, you pick one of three colors and you're done. No category lookup, no decision tree.

**Resilience.** A simple system survives a bad week. The elaborate system doesn't — it requires the energy that bad weeks consume.

**Lower cognitive overhead.** The system should serve the user, not the other way around. A complex system requires maintaining knowledge of the system itself.

## What simplicity doesn't cost

Simplicity doesn't mean less information. A calendar with three colors and specific task names in block titles contains more usable information than a calendar with twelve categories and vague event titles.

Simplicity doesn't mean less planning. A 20-minute weekly review is simple. It's also powerful. The simplicity is in the structure, not the quality of thinking.

Simplicity doesn't mean less protection. A Busy-status morning block is structurally simple. It's also effective at doing the one thing it's supposed to do.

## The audit question

When evaluating any calendar element, ask: does this make the calendar faster to read and easier to use? If no, remove it. If the answer is uncertain, try removing it and see if anything breaks.

> Most calendar systems that have become complicated got that way through addition without subtraction. The path back is systematic removal of what no longer earns its place.

---

**This week's takeaway:**
- Simple calendars are more readable, more maintainable, and more resilient than complex ones
- Simplicity is about structure, not information — simple structure + specific names = most useful calendar
- Audit question: does this element make the calendar faster to use? If not, consider removing it
