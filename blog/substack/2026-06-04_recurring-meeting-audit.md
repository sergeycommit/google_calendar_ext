# The Recurring Meeting Audit: I Cancelled 40% of Mine

*Schedule Calendar Weekly · August 21, 2026*

---

Recurring meetings are the subscription boxes of work life. You sign up, they deliver, and most months you wonder why you still have them. Unlike subscriptions, they're also claiming your time twice: once when they run, and once every other hour of the week when they take up space in your mental calendar.

I did a proper audit last quarter. The number that stuck with me: 40% of my recurring meetings were no longer serving their original purpose. They were still happening because nobody had explicitly stopped them.

## Why recurring meetings outlive their purpose

When a meeting is created, there's usually a specific reason. A new project launches and needs a weekly sync. A problem emerges that requires regular check-ins. A team is forming and needs coordination.

Then the project ends, the problem gets solved, the team stabilizes — and the meeting keeps running. Nobody cancels it because nobody wants to be the one who implies the coordination isn't needed. The meeting lives in institutional inertia.

## The audit process

Set aside 45 minutes and pull up your full recurring meeting list. For each one, ask three questions:

1. **What would break if this meeting stopped tomorrow?** Be specific. "We'd lose alignment" doesn't count. "The team wouldn't know about blocking issues until Friday" does.

2. **Could the value be achieved with less frequency?** A weekly that became bi-weekly reduces load by 50% without eliminating the coordination.

3. **Is the current invite list still the right one?** Projects change. People's roles change. An 8-person meeting that originally needed everyone might now need 4.

## The action categories

After applying these questions, each meeting fell into one of four categories for me:

**Keep as-is:** Meetings I couldn't reduce or change without real cost. About 35% of my recurring events.

**Reduce frequency:** Weekly → bi-weekly. Monthly → quarterly. About 25%.

**Reduce scope:** Shorter duration or smaller invite list, same cadence. About 15%.

**Cancel:** No specific reason to continue. About 25%.

> The hardest part is cancelling a meeting you organize. There's a social dynamic around it. What helped: framing it as "let's try pausing this for a month and see what we miss" rather than a permanent cancellation. Rarely does anyone push back. Rarely does the meeting get revived.

---

**This week's takeaway:**
- Recurring meetings accumulate on inertia, not ongoing need
- Ask "what specifically breaks if this stops?" — vague value claims don't justify the time
- Reduce frequency before cancelling; it's an easier first step and often good enough

*Block 45 minutes this week for a recurring meeting audit. The ROI is immediate.*
