# An Engineer's Guide to Treating Their Calendar Like a Codebase

*Schedule Calendar Weekly · July 9, 2027*

---

Engineers spend a lot of effort on system design: clear interfaces, minimal coupling, thoughtful abstractions. The best codebases are deliberate — every component has a clear purpose, the whole is understandable, and changes are made intentionally.

Most engineers I know apply zero of this thinking to their calendars. The result is the scheduling equivalent of spaghetti code: meetings scheduled reactively, no consistent patterns, high context-switching cost, and weekly reviews that produce confusion instead of clarity.

## Applying engineering principles to scheduling

**Separation of concerns.** Deep work, meetings, shallow work, and personal time are different concern domains. They should be clearly separated on the calendar — different colors, different time windows — not interleaved randomly. When everything looks the same, you can't read the schedule at a glance any more than you can read code with no formatting.

**Consistent interfaces.** A meeting invitation is an interface. A well-structured invite has a clear purpose, the right attendees, an agenda, and an expected output. A vague invite is a poorly documented function: you have to trace into the implementation to understand what it does.

**Reduce state.** Carrying too many open calendar commitments creates context-switching load analogous to too much mutable state. Recurring meetings that have outlived their purpose are technical debt. Audit them.

**The single responsibility principle.** Calendar blocks work better when they have one clear purpose. A "meeting/focus/standup/planning" block is a class doing too much. Name it specifically.

## The specific calendar structure that works for engineers

Most engineers have: high-concentration coding or design work that needs 90-120 uninterrupted minutes, team coordination that can happen in short synchronous bursts, and administrative overhead (PR reviews, documentation, email) that can be batched.

The calendar that works: two 90-minute deep work blocks in the morning, meeting windows in the afternoon, one shallow work batch at end of day. Simple, repeatable, defensible.

> Your calendar is a system you interact with every day. It's worth the same design effort you'd put into a system others interact with every day.

- Two morning deep work blocks, afternoon meetings, end-of-day shallow work batch: a clean engineering schedule structure
