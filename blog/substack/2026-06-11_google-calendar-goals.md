# Can Your Calendar Actually Help You Achieve Goals? I Tested It.

*Schedule Calendar Weekly · October 9, 2026*

---

Every January, I write a list of goals. Every December, I look at it and feel the particular discomfort of goals that were never scheduled.

Not because I forgot about them. Because I never gave them calendar time. They were intentions — which is a different thing from plans.

Last year I tried an experiment: every major goal got a regular calendar block, and the block had a specific name that connected back to the goal. The results were not surprising once you think about it: the goals with calendar time mostly happened. The ones without it mostly didn't.

## Google Calendar's Goals feature

Google Calendar has a built-in Goals feature (under the + menu in mobile, "Goals" section in settings on desktop) that automatically finds time for repeating personal goals — exercise, reading, learning a language, meditation. It reschedules itself when conflicts arise.

It's genuinely useful for habit-type goals with flexible timing. Want to exercise four times per week? Set the goal, let Calendar find the slots.

The limitation: it works best for goals that fit in fixed-duration, repeating sessions without complex dependencies. For project goals or career goals, you need a different approach.

## The manual version for bigger goals

For goals that require sustained work over time — a course you want to finish, a project you want to launch, a skill you want to develop — I use a different method:

**Break the goal into milestones.** "Launch new service" becomes: research done, offer drafted, landing page live, first 3 clients.

**Assign each milestone a target date.** Work backward from the goal deadline.

**Block weekly time for the goal.** A 60–90 minute recurring block, titled with the goal name, every week until the milestone date.

The recurring block becomes a visible commitment. It shows up alongside meetings. It competes for time like everything else — which is exactly what it should do, because it's no less important than most of what fills the calendar.

> The calendar doesn't achieve goals for you. But it does make the gap between "I want to do this" and "I have time to do this" visible and undeniable.

---

**This week's takeaway:**
- Goals without calendar time are intentions — scheduling them is what turns them into plans
- Google Calendar's Goals feature works well for flexible habit-type goals with daily/weekly repetition
- For bigger project goals, work backward from a deadline and create weekly blocks for the work
