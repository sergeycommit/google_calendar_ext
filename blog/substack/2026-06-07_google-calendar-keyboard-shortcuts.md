# 25 Seconds Saved Per Calendar Interaction. Per Day, That's 8 Minutes.

*Schedule Calendar Weekly · September 11, 2026*

---

I know, I know. Eight minutes. That doesn't sound like a lot. But if you check your calendar 20 times a day — which is conservative — and each check takes 15 seconds instead of 40, you've reclaimed 500 seconds. That compounds.

More importantly, the thing that gets eliminated isn't just time. It's friction. When navigating the calendar is effortless, you actually look at it before scheduling something. You check for conflicts. You see the shape of your week before committing to more of it.

## The shortcuts worth knowing cold

I've tried to use every keyboard shortcut in Google Calendar at some point. Most are forgettable. These five are not:

**T:** Jump to today's view instantly, from any date you've drifted to.

**1, 2, 3, 4:** Switch between Day, Week, Month, and 4-day views without touching the toolbar.

**Q:** Quick event creation. Start typing and the natural language parser often does the rest.

**E:** Opens an event for editing when your cursor is on it.

**Backspace:** Deletes the selected event.

Learn those five and you'll cover 80% of daily calendar navigation. Everything else is bonus.

## The ones that save time in specific workflows

**C:** Create a new event (more control than Q).

**S:** Weekly summary view — useful for reviewing the next 7 days in one shot.

**D:** Day view toggle — when you want to see a specific day in detail.

**Forward/back arrows:** Navigate weeks or months without reaching for the mouse.

## Why most people don't use shortcuts

The answer is simple: nobody told them the shortcuts exist. Google Calendar's shortcut panel (accessible via the ? key) shows all available shortcuts, but most users discover it years into using the product, if ever.

The other reason: the interface is already fast enough that the shortcuts feel optional. And they are optional — until you check your calendar fifty times a day and the friction starts adding up.

> Try one new shortcut per week rather than all of them at once. Muscle memory takes repetition, not willpower. One shortcut per week is six new habits in a month.
