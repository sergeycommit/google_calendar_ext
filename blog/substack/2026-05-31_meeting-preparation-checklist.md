# The 5-Minute Pre-Meeting Ritual That Changes How Meetings Go

*Schedule Calendar Weekly · July 24, 2026*

---

Most people prepare for important meetings. Almost nobody prepares for routine ones. And since most meetings feel routine — a recurring sync, a project update, a check-in — most meetings start cold.

Cold-start meetings are identifiable: the first few minutes are spent confirming what the meeting is about, who owns which agenda item, and what happened since last time. That's 5–10 minutes of a 30-minute call doing nothing useful.

Five minutes of preparation before joining eliminates most of this.

## What the five minutes should cover

**Review the agenda.** Even if you wrote it, skim it again. Remind yourself of the specific outcome you want.

**Note your key points.** Two or three things you need to say, question, or decide. Write them down before you join. This prevents the frustration of remembering what you wanted to say 20 minutes after the call ends.

**Check relevant context.** Open the doc, ticket, or shared resource the meeting will reference. Being in the right mental space when the call starts removes minutes of context switching during it.

**Set your status.** If you're in focus mode before the meeting, a 3-minute buffer lets you close the current work, mark yourself available, and arrive mentally present.

## What happens without preparation

Meetings without preparation run longer, produce fuzzier outcomes, and create follow-up chains to clarify things that should have been decided in the call. The 30-minute meeting that "needs a follow-up" is usually a meeting that needed 5 more minutes of prep.

The people who consistently run tight, productive meetings almost always prepare. It's not talent — it's the discipline of treating the meeting as a deliverable, not just a time block.

> One useful practice: when a meeting ends, immediately schedule a 5-minute block before your next meeting for prep. Make the prep visible in the calendar, not just an intention.

## For meetings you didn't organize

You don't need a formal agenda to prepare. Read the invite description. Look at who else is attending. Ask yourself: what's the most useful thing I could contribute in this meeting? Having that answer before you join changes how you show up.

---

**This week's takeaway:**
- 5 minutes of preparation before a meeting eliminates most of the dead time at the start
- Write down your key points before joining — it prevents post-meeting regret
- Buffer blocks between meetings create structural prep time, not just breathing room

*Before your next three meetings: spend 5 minutes on prep and notice whether the meeting runs differently.*
