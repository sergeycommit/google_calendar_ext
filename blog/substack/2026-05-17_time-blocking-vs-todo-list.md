# To-Do Lists Lie. Here's What They're Missing.

*Schedule Calendar Weekly · April 17, 2026*

---

My to-do list told me I could do twelve things on Tuesday. My calendar told me I had four hours of meetings, two errands, and a doctor's appointment.

The list was lying. Not maliciously — it just didn't know about the calendar. And the calendar didn't know about the list. Neither one had the full picture, so I spent most of Tuesday feeling behind on things I technically had no time to do.

## What to-do lists are actually good at

A to-do list is a capture tool. Low friction to add items. Clean view of what's outstanding. Easy to reorder by priority. It holds everything that needs to happen without requiring you to decide *when*.

That's its strength. That's also its failure mode. The list never tells you whether you have time for its items. It just sits there accumulating, and the gap between what's on the list and what the calendar can actually hold is where a lot of stress lives.

## What calendar blocks are actually good at

A calendar block gives a task a home in time. It answers: not just what needs doing, but when, and for how long. When you put a task on the calendar alongside your meetings, you can see immediately whether the week can hold it.

The weakness: maintaining a tightly blocked calendar takes effort. Unexpected items displace blocks. Blocking everything creates rigidity that makes the week feel like a constraint rather than a plan.

## The combined system that works

Neither tool alone is the answer. Here's the system I settled on:

**Once a week (Sunday or Friday):** Review the task list and block time for items that *must* happen this week. Not everything needs a block — only the tasks that require focused work or have a deadline. The list is infinite; the calendar is finite. That tension is the point. It forces real tradeoffs.

**Each day:** Within a work block, use the task list as a queue. The calendar determines *when* you're working on something; the list determines *what* you work on within that window.

**End of day:** Sync what moved. Which tasks didn't happen? Do they need a new block next week, or can they wait for the next review?

> The insight: the list and the calendar solve different halves of the same problem. The list says "what." The calendar says "when." You need both to answer "will this actually get done."

---

**This week's takeaway:**
- To-do lists capture; calendar blocks schedule
- Use the weekly review to move list items into actual calendar blocks
- Let the calendar reveal the gap between what you want to do and what time you actually have
